The problem with tool-using models is usually how novelty can impersonate depth

An essay on tool-using models, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

My notes on tool-using models began with a small irritation in March 2022. The easy agreement around the big story felt like a warning, not a comfort. When consensus arrives that quickly, I assume some important rough edge has been sanded off to make it travel better. In this case the omitted piece was how novelty can impersonate depth. The abstract version vanishes as soon as you track who has to maintain the thing, who bears its failures, and who gains from the trust it extracts.

Tool-using models appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. Engineering makes for easier conversation because it offers parts, systems, metrics, and a tidy performance surface. The more speculative part is harder to narrate, but it matters just as much. Each tool contains a quiet model of the user and the kind of society that should feel normal around it. Those buried assumptions show up quite clearly in the choices the design makes. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also a question of what sorts of habits and dependencies the system makes feel ordinary.

I kept noticing the moral distance between a prototype and the public environment it eventually enters. Inside a prototype, everyone knows they are participating in a test. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. The first audience is often self-selected for novelty, which makes the test environment gentler than public life. It is much riskier to move the same logic into schools, hospitals, transit, housing, or workplaces and assume a sleek interface will do the moral work. How novelty can impersonate depth becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

My taste for the subject stayed mostly intact, but my threshold for abstraction fell sharply. After writing “What telepresence rituals taught me about building futures,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. Any claim about the future that collapses on contact with ordinary life does not earn much trust from me. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I am after is not purity so much as proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. Still, it matters to notice when a useful tool crosses the line into quiet obligation. Tool-using models seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Most of the time it becomes real first as a relationship style, not as a cleanly named invention.

A final note to myself from March 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The future has become, for me, a way of tracking what a system requires from the people beside the work. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. Flashiness has a way of carrying attention away from what matters.
