I keep coming back to telepresence rituals

A personal essay about telepresence rituals, and the longer argument hidden inside how coordination can feel intimate and brutal at the same time.

I wrote this down in August 2020 after thinking about telepresence rituals from the vantage point of the unnerving quiet of a masked grocery line. The better question, for me, was not the headline one but the one underneath it: what kind of life is this idea assuming? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. We keep prioritizing capability, performance, and scale while deferring the question of whether the system improves daily life. Telepresence rituals seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. Public language likes the visible layer, the robot, the interface, the model, the thing that screenshots well. Most tools matter less because of the demo and more because of the secondary effects they set loose. They change pacing. They decide who can go on operating through judgment and who must become parseable. They make different things visible or ignorable because the workflow beneath the surface has shifted. Once you notice that shift, telepresence rituals stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

I do not think atmosphere is a soft concern. It is where many technologies tell the truth about themselves. Some leave people feeling more capable and less managed. Others quietly convert daily life into a set of nudges, checks, and approvals. That effect may not show up in a benchmark chart, but it absolutely shows up in the lived experience of the system.

Looking back from August 2020, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “A note on public health dashboards after convenience turned into dependency,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. The applause had stopped being the interesting part. The interesting part was the burden that arrived after it. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was incomplete in the familiar way optimism becomes incomplete before the machinery has touched enough lives.

I do not see withdrawal from technology as the right lesson here. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Useful tools should invite scrutiny too, especially about the world they normalize. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version keeps the frame narrow until the world forces it wider. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On repair culture before venture language flattened the mood.”

A final note to myself from August 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I do not find the argument about what comes next as gripping as the pressure a tool puts on the people living beside it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
