On evaluation while everyone tried to make software feel responsible

A reflection on evaluation and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in February 2024 after thinking about evaluation from the vantage point of a whiteboard full of edge cases rather than aspirations. The headline claim interested me less than the older question below it: what sort of world is this idea already presuming? Around technology, that is usually the first thing to get edited out. We keep prioritizing capability, performance, and scale while deferring the question of whether the system improves daily life. Evaluation seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. In public, the visible part gets all the attention, the robot, the interface, the model, the shiny layer on top. Technologies tend to matter socially because of the surrounding effects, not just the headline function. They change pacing. They decide who can go on operating through judgment and who must become parseable. They redraw the boundary of neglect by routing more things through hidden workflows. Once you notice that shift, evaluation stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The atmosphere around a tool is not decorative, it is part of the thing being built. Some systems create room for judgment and improvisation. Others turn ordinary life into a corridor of tiny permissions. That shift can be difficult to benchmark, but it is impossible to live inside without noticing. The mood around a system tells you something real about its politics.

Looking back from February 2024, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “I keep coming back to local inference,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. At that point I was still describing what looked appealing on the surface. The real subject had shifted for me from the performance to the obligations that trailed behind it. I would not call that earlier optimism wrong, exactly. It was a version of optimism that had not yet survived enough proximity.

None of this adds up to a case for becoming anti-technology. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. It should be possible to like a tool and still question the world it is making normal. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version keeps the frame narrow until the world forces it wider. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “The problem with delivery logistics is usually software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit.”

A final note to myself from February 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I am not as captivated by what comes next as by the pressure a tool puts on those beside it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The polished version is often exactly how the crucial part gets missed.
