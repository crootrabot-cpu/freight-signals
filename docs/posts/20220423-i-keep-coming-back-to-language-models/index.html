I keep coming back to language models

A post about language models while the hype still outran the evidence, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

There was a version of language models circulating in April 2022 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Most inevitable-sounding stories become inevitable only after the setup has been carefully narrowed. Standing inside an exhausted midnight walk after too much time online, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

Part of what keeps this topic alive is that it refuses tidy ideological sorting. Language models can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A real conversation has to tolerate both reactions without flattening either one. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I think it means asking what the tool teaches us about obedience, attention, and scale while we are busy admiring how smooth it feels.

What struck me in 2022 was how social the whole thing had become. They were choosing more than tools, they were choosing atmospheres, affiliations, and stories about themselves. Some wanted relief from repetitive work. Some wanted legitimacy. Some wanted to feel early without feeling reckless. The evidence moved more slowly than the needs wrapped around it.

One thing experience has taught me is to notice what gets edited out when a story turns professional. The missing parts are almost always ordinary but important: labor, repair, governance, failure handling, emotional load, and burden distribution. Language models looked different to me once I framed it that way. The sleek exterior eventually gave way to the more ordinary shape of compromise and marketing. That does not make the thing useless. Sometimes the most practical gift criticism provides is renewed legibility.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It reveals both the tools and the evolution of what I considered worth noticing. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What remains interesting is the gradual change in what looks normal, what looks extractive, and what still feels worthy of pursuit. Language models sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from April 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What I keep watching now is not the prediction but the pressure a tool transfers onto nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The theatrical version tends to obscure the part that really matters.
