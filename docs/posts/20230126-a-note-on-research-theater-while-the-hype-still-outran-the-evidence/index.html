A note on research theater while the hype still outran the evidence

An essay on research theater, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in January 2023 after thinking about research theater from the vantage point of a quiet hour reading papers with too many grand nouns. What held me was not the headline but the deeper question underneath it: what sort of world is this idea written for? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. We talk about capability before context, performance before maintenance, and scale before asking whether any of it makes life more livable. Research theater seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The socially important mechanism was not the one public explanations liked to point at first. In public, the visible part gets all the attention, the robot, the interface, the model, the shiny layer on top. Most tools matter less because of the demo and more because of the secondary effects they set loose. They change pacing. They alter who may stay flexible and who must start performing legibility for the machine. They redraw the boundary of neglect by routing more things through hidden workflows. Once you notice that shift, research theater stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is a version of this argument that ignores atmosphere, and I do not trust it. Some systems open up breathing room. Others make every action feel slightly supervised. Measurement rarely captures the difference cleanly, but daily life does. When a tool thickens procedure and shrinks the room people feel around themselves, that belongs in the evaluation.

Looking back from January 2023, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “I keep coming back to tool-using models,” from November 2022. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. What held me by then was not the performance but the aftermath, the maintenance work, the politics, the sorting logic, and the emotional terms of use. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was incomplete in the familiar way optimism becomes incomplete before the machinery has touched enough lives.

The goal, to me, is not anti-technology purity. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. A good tool still deserves questions about the world it makes easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by refusing the oversized story until the smaller one earns the right to expand. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “The problem with contactless interfaces is usually the difference between resilience and improvisation.”

A final note to myself from January 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What stays in focus for me now is not the headline forecast but the burden a tool pushes onto ordinary people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
