What prompt rituals taught me about building futures

An essay on prompt rituals, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in May 2022 after thinking about prompt rituals from the vantage point of a browser window full of generated images. The better question, for me, was not the headline one but the one underneath it: what kind of life is this idea assuming? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. Most conversations rush from capability to performance to scale while the livability question trails behind. Prompt rituals seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The part that mattered to me was not the mechanism people tended to highlight in public. In public, people usually start with the visible layer, the dashboard, the model, the robot, the part that demos cleanly. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a generalist and who has to become legible to the machine. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, prompt rituals stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The question of atmosphere keeps drawing me back because metrics tend to leave it out. One system leaves room for human judgment. Another makes the whole environment feel permissioned, prompted, and monitored. That difference may be hard to quantify, but it is easy to feel. The emotional climate around a tool is part of the thing it makes.

Looking back from May 2022, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “The problem with tool-using models is usually how novelty can impersonate depth,” from March 2022. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. I had become more interested in the system after the applause than during it, especially in the maintenance and social consequences that came next. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. The optimism arrived before the machinery had earned its full share of lived consequences.

The goal, to me, is not anti-technology purity. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can admire good tools without giving up the right to ask what kind of world they are quietly assembling. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by resisting the big story until the smaller one proves itself durable. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “A note on mutual aid apps after convenience turned into dependency.”

A final note to myself from May 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point being right about the future matters less than being honest about who carries the burden. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
