On model demos as the internet learned a new kind of performance

An essay on model demos, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

There was a version of model demos circulating in August 2022 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Most inevitable-sounding stories become inevitable only after the setup has been carefully narrowed. Standing inside a quiet hour reading papers with too many grand nouns, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

It keeps drawing me back because it won’t settle politely into ideological categories. Model demos can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. The mature version of this argument has to make room for both sides of that feeling. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool normalizes while everyone is busy applauding the convenience.

In 2022, I could not separate the technical story from the social one. These systems were practical, yes, but they were also vehicles for aspiration, identity, and fatigue. People wanted different things from them: less drudgery, more status, clearer judgment, fewer boring tasks. The problem was that social desire kept moving faster than the underlying evidence.

One thing experience has taught me is to notice what gets edited out when a story turns professional. Most professional retellings drop the same things, labor, governance, repair, emotional cost, and who pays when the system misbehaves. Model demos looked different to me once I framed it that way. The more I looked, the less it resembled a solution and the more it resembled persuasion attached to trade-offs. That does not make the thing useless. Sometimes criticism is valuable simply because it makes the object legible again.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It offers a record not only of the tools but of the habits of attention I was slowly acquiring. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The part that lasts is the slower movement in what becomes normal, what becomes extractive, and what continues to feel worth wanting. Model demos sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from August 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I care less about being right about the future than about what a tool quietly asks of the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The prettier version is often the one that helps us miss the important thing.
