The problem with remote presence is usually software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit

A post about remote presence after convenience turned into dependency, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

There was a version of remote presence circulating in July 2021 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. It is usually a story told after somebody has quietly selected the conditions that make the trend look obvious. Standing inside the unnerving quiet of a masked grocery line, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

One reason the topic stays interesting is that it will not settle into a clean ideological camp. Remote presence can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Keeping both reactions in view is what makes the conversation serious. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. For me the question is what the tool trains us to accept while we are busy praising the low-friction experience.

What struck me in 2021 was how social the whole thing had become. What they were choosing was not just tooling but a whole atmosphere and a flattering self-description. Some wanted relief from repetitive work. Some wanted legitimacy. Some wanted to feel early without feeling reckless. Social need outran evidence again and again.

Experience keeps pushing me toward the same habit: notice what disappears in the polished retelling. It is usually the same stack of missing details: labor, repair, governance, emotional burden, error handling, and uneven cost. Remote presence looked different to me once I framed it that way. The elegant solution started to look less elegant and more like negotiated compromise wrapped in nice copy. That does not make the thing useless. Before criticism does anything grander, it often just restores readability.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It lets me see not only what the technologies were doing but also what I was training myself to notice. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What remains interesting is the gradual change in what looks normal, what looks extractive, and what still feels worthy of pursuit. Remote presence sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from July 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The argument about what comes next grips me less than the pressure a tool places on the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The theatrical version tends to obscure the part that really matters.
