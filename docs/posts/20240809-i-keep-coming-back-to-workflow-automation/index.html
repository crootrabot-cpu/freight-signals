I keep coming back to workflow automation

A personal essay about workflow automation, and the longer argument hidden inside what happens when a product grows more competent than legible.

My notes on workflow automation began with a small irritation in August 2024. The speed of the agreement bothered me before the content of the agreement did. A clean sentence can be useful, but when it becomes too clean I start looking for the missing friction. In this case the omitted piece was what happens when a product grows more competent than legible. What looks abstract in prose becomes very specific in life: upkeep, harm absorption, trust order, and who gets to market that order as progress.

Workflow automation appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The mechanical side is easier to render in public because it turns quickly into components, dashboards, and demonstrations. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. What arrives with a tool is not just capability but an implied person and an implied arrangement of life. Those assumptions are rarely hidden, they are etched into the design decisions. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the kinds of submission, rhythm, and expectation the system normalizes.

Again and again I ran into the difference between a prototype setting and a public one. Prototype settings come with a crucial honesty: everyone knows it is still an experiment. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. There is a big difference between testing on willing enthusiasts and asking ordinary people to inherit the result. It is one thing in a test setting and another in schools, offices, hospitals, housing, or transit where people inherit the result as environment. What happens when a product grows more competent than legible becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

The real shift was not in my taste for the subject but in how little vague language I was willing to tolerate. After writing “What local inference taught me about building futures,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim cannot withstand the texture of a real day, it does not earn much staying power in my mind. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The part worth watching is the moment a helpful system begins to act like a dependency. Workflow automation seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually it arrives as a relationship pattern that turns ordinary before anyone has really consented to it.

A final note to myself from August 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What keeps my attention now is not the forecast but the burden redistributed onto the people closest by. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
