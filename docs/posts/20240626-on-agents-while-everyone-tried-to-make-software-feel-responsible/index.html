On agents while everyone tried to make software feel responsible

A personal essay about agents, and the longer argument hidden inside the strange politics of delegating judgment.

There was a version of agents circulating in June 2024 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. It is usually a story told after somebody has quietly selected the conditions that make the trend look obvious. Standing inside a whiteboard full of edge cases rather than aspirations, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

Part of what keeps this topic alive is that it refuses tidy ideological sorting. Agents can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Any adult conversation about it has to hold those two responses together. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool normalizes while everyone is busy applauding the convenience.

What struck me in 2024 was how social the whole thing had become. People were not merely picking tools. They were also picking affiliations, atmospheres, and versions of themselves. Some wanted relief from repetitive work. Some wanted legitimacy. Some wanted to feel early without feeling reckless. Those social needs moved faster than the evidence ever did.

One thing experience has taught me is to notice what gets edited out when a story turns professional. Most of the absent material lives in the same neighborhood: labor, maintenance, emotional cost, repair, governance, and asymmetric inconvenience. Agents looked different to me once I framed it that way. The elegant solution started to look less elegant and more like negotiated compromise wrapped in nice copy. That does not make the thing useless. It mostly makes the thing legible again, which is often the first real service criticism provides.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It gives me a record of the tools and of the standards by which I was learning to judge them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What remains interesting is the gradual change in what looks normal, what looks extractive, and what still feels worthy of pursuit. Agents sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from June 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I am more interested in the obligations a tool creates than in the prestige of a correct forecast. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The shiny version is often the easiest way to overlook what actually matters.
