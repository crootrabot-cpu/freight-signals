What homebound automation taught me about building futures

A post about homebound automation when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

I wrote this down in May 2020 after thinking about homebound automation from the vantage point of a video call where everyone looked slightly ghosted by their own webcam. The headline claim interested me less than the older question below it: what sort of world is this idea already presuming? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. We talk about capability before context, performance before maintenance, and scale before asking whether any of it makes life more livable. Homebound automation seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. In public, the visible part gets all the attention, the robot, the interface, the model, the shiny layer on top. The part that becomes socially important is usually the second-order shift around the technology. They change pacing. They decide who can go on operating through judgment and who must become parseable. They make different things visible or ignorable because the workflow beneath the surface has shifted. Once you notice that shift, homebound automation stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The atmosphere around a tool is not decorative, it is part of the thing being built. Some systems create room for judgment and improvisation. Others turn ordinary life into a corridor of tiny permissions. That shift can be difficult to benchmark, but it is impossible to live inside without noticing. The mood around a system tells you something real about its politics.

Looking back from May 2020, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “The problem with fragile supply chains is usually the difference between resilience and improvisation,” from January 2020. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. The real subject had shifted for me from the performance to the obligations that trailed behind it. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. The optimism needed more contact with actual use before it could become wisdom.

I do not think the answer is to become anti-technology as a form of self-protection. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. There is no contradiction between appreciating a tool and interrogating the environment it builds. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by resisting the big story until the smaller one proves itself durable. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “A note on human-scale robotics as an art project with civic consequences.”

A final note to myself from May 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point the burden a tool places on nearby people matters more to me than the abstract future it points toward. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
