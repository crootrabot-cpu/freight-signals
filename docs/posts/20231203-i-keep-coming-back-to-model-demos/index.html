I keep coming back to model demos

A personal essay about model demos, and the longer argument hidden inside how novelty can impersonate depth.

I wrote this down in December 2023 after thinking about model demos from the vantage point of a browser window full of generated images. The surface topic mattered less than the background assumption about the world the idea expects to find. Technology conversations usually lose that part first. Most conversations rush from capability to performance to scale while the livability question trails behind. Model demos seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. The public version usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, robot, control surface. Social importance tends to arrive through side effects that stop feeling like side effects. They change pacing. They redraw the line between people who can stay broad and people who must become machine-readable. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, model demos stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

I circle back to atmosphere because it holds onto details metrics drop. One system leaves room for human judgment. Another makes everything feel like prompts plus a stack of tiny permissions. It may be hard to quantify, but it registers quickly at the level of feeling. A tool’s output includes the mood it creates around ordinary life.

Looking back from December 2023, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “A note on image models while the hype still outran the evidence,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. That earlier note was still occupied with the thing’s surface appeal. What held me by then was not the performance but the aftermath, the maintenance work, the politics, the sorting logic, and the emotional terms of use. The earlier optimism was not exactly wrong to my mind. It was optimism with too little lived contact behind it.

None of this adds up to a case for becoming anti-technology. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. It should be possible to like a tool and still question the world it is making normal. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It begins by distrusting the inflated version until the smaller account earns more room. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On coordination systems when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite.”

A final note to myself from December 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What matters to me in the future tense is what the system extracts from the people closest to the work. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The shiny version is often the easiest way to overlook what actually matters.
