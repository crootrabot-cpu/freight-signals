I keep coming back to benchmarks

A post about benchmarks as the internet learned a new kind of performance, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

I wrote this down in April 2023 after thinking about benchmarks from the vantage point of a browser window full of generated images. I kept drifting past the headline toward the quieter question beneath it, what kind of world does this idea imagine waiting for it? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. Most conversations rush from capability to performance to scale while the livability question trails behind. Benchmarks seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. In public, people usually start with the visible layer, the dashboard, the model, the robot, the part that demos cleanly. A technology’s social life is often written in its second-order effects rather than its headline pitch. They change pacing. They redraw the line between people who can stay broad and people who must become machine-readable. They change what can be ignored because a hidden workflow is now catching it. Once you notice that shift, benchmarks stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the older question of atmosphere. Some tools widen the room around a person. Others make every surface feel like an input field. That distinction does not show up neatly in a benchmark, but it shapes daily life anyway. When a system leaves people feeling watched, hurried, or permission-bound, that is not just collateral. It is part of what the system is making normal.

Looking back from April 2023, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “On AI search when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary,” from February 2023. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. By then the interesting part was the system’s afterlife, not its stage presence. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was optimism with too little lived contact behind it.

I do not think the answer is to become anti-technology as a form of self-protection. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. There is no contradiction between appreciating a tool and interrogating the environment it builds. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by resisting the big story until the smaller one proves itself durable. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “I keep coming back to telepresence rituals.”

A final note to myself from April 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I do not find the argument about what comes next as gripping as the pressure a tool puts on the people living beside it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
