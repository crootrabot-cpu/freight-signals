I keep coming back to image models

A reflection on image models and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in July 2022 after thinking about image models from the vantage point of an exhausted midnight walk after too much time online. I kept drifting past the headline toward the quieter question beneath it, what kind of world does this idea imagine waiting for it? That is often the first thing technology makes harder to see. The usual sequence favors capability over context, performance over upkeep, and scale over livability. Image models seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The part that mattered to me was not the mechanism people tended to highlight in public. Public talk tends to reach first for the visible move, the interface, the model, the robot, the service layer. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a generalist and who has to become legible to the machine. They change what gets missed and what gets caught by moving the work into hidden systems. Once you notice that shift, image models stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the older question of atmosphere. Some tools widen the room around a person. Others make every surface feel like an input field. That distinction does not show up neatly in a benchmark, but it shapes daily life anyway. When a system leaves people feeling watched, hurried, or permission-bound, that is not just collateral. It is part of what the system is making normal.

Looking back from July 2022, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “A note on chat interfaces before the story settled,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. Back in that earlier piece, I was still focused on the surface attraction. What held me by then was not the performance but the aftermath, the maintenance work, the politics, the sorting logic, and the emotional terms of use. I still would not say the earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was an optimism that had not yet met enough life to understand its own limits.

Anti-technology as self-protection does not strike me as the answer. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Useful tools should invite scrutiny too, especially about the world they normalize. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The better habit is to hold the story modest until the evidence asks for something larger. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “I keep coming back to warehouse software.”

A final note to myself from July 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point the burden a tool places on nearby people matters more to me than the abstract future it points toward. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The flashy presentation is often what nudges attention off the real issue.
