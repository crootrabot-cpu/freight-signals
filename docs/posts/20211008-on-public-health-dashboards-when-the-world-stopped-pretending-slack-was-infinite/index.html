On public health dashboards when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite

A personal essay about public health dashboards, and the longer argument hidden inside how a crisis reveals which systems were decorative.

There was a version of public health dashboards circulating in October 2021 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Usually that kind of inevitability appears only after someone has chosen a very flattering frame. Standing inside an empty downtown street, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep circling back to it because it refuses clean ideological categories. Public health dashboards can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Any adult conversation about it has to hold those two responses together. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. To me it means asking what habits of obedience and attention the tool installs while we celebrate the convenience.

By 2021, the systems were doing double duty as tools and as social signals. People wanted them not only for their usefulness but for the flattering self-image they offered. That is where my caution kicked in. Once a system starts doing identity work, the claims around it tend to expand ahead of the proof.

The longer I do this, the more I watch the details that fall away once the polished retelling begins. Usually the missing pieces come from the same family: labor, error handling, emotional load, repair, governance, and unevenly distributed inconvenience. Public health dashboards looked different to me once I framed it that way. It stopped reading like a clean breakthrough and started reading like managed trade-offs. That does not make the thing useless. Before criticism does anything grander, it often just restores readability.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It gives me a record of the tools and of the standards by which I was learning to judge them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The interesting part is the slower rearrangement of what feels ordinary, what feels predatory, and what still feels worth asking for. Public health dashboards sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from October 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I do not find the argument about what comes next as compelling as the pressure a tool puts on the people living beside it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The prettier version is often the one that helps us miss the important thing.
