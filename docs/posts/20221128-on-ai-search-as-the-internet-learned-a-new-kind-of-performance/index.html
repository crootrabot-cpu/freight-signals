On AI search as the internet learned a new kind of performance

A personal essay about AI search, and the longer argument hidden inside the pressure to form a worldview too quickly.

I wrote this down in November 2022 after thinking about AI search from the vantage point of a quiet hour reading papers with too many grand nouns. The surface topic mattered less than the background assumption about the world the idea expects to find. With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. The order is almost always capability first, then performance, then scale, and only later the question of livability. AI search seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The part that mattered to me was not the mechanism people tended to highlight in public. Public language likes the visible layer, the robot, the interface, the model, the thing that screenshots well. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a generalist and who has to become legible to the machine. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, AI search stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

I come back to atmosphere because it captures what the measured view tends to miss. One system leaves room for human judgment. Another makes the whole environment feel permissioned, prompted, and monitored. That difference may be hard to quantify, but it is easy to feel. What a tool makes includes the mood it leaves people inside.

Looking back from November 2022, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “I keep coming back to tool-using models,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. The applause had stopped being the interesting part. The interesting part was the burden that arrived after it. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was optimism with too little lived contact behind it.

I do not think the answer is to become anti-technology as a form of self-protection. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Admiration does not require silence about the world a tool is slowly constructing around itself. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It begins by distrusting the inflated version until the smaller account earns more room. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “What homebound automation taught me about building futures.”

A final note to myself from November 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I do not find the argument about what comes next as gripping as the pressure a tool puts on the people living beside it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
