On telepresence rituals under pressure

A post about telepresence rituals under pressure, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

My notes on telepresence rituals began with a small irritation in January 2021. The consensus had arrived so cleanly that it felt less like clarity and more like omission. Consensus that comes too quickly often hides a quiet editing process underneath it. In this case the omitted piece was the difference between resilience and improvisation. In real settings it stops sounding abstract and starts sounding like maintenance, blame, social risk, and carefully distributed advantage.

Telepresence rituals appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The engineering story is easy to tell because it arrives with visible pieces and measurable claims. The imagined side is harder to formalize, but it matters no less. What arrives with a tool is not just capability but an implied person and an implied arrangement of life. Those assumptions become visible the moment you stop taking the design at face value. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the style of dependence the system teaches while everyone is busy calling it useful.

I kept coming back to the distance between prototype life and public life. The prototype has one advantage, nobody mistakes it for settled reality. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Testing among willing novelty-seekers is one thing. It is another matter to embed that logic in ordinary institutions and expect people to thank you for polishing the surface. The difference between resilience and improvisation becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

What changed most was not my interest in the topic but my tolerance for abstraction. After writing “A note on public health dashboards after convenience turned into dependency,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim cannot withstand the texture of a real day, it does not earn much staying power in my mind. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

I am not after purity. I am after proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. I mostly want us to notice the slide from convenience into obligation. Telepresence rituals seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. More often the shift is relational before it is technological, and it becomes normal before it becomes examined.

A final note to myself from January 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I care less about being right about the future than about what a tool quietly asks of the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The sleek version is often how the essential part slips past us.
