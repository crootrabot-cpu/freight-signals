On telepresence rituals under pressure

A shorter, rougher note from early 2021 about telepresence rituals, fatigue, and the pressure to make remote life sound cleaner than it felt.

My notes on telepresence rituals began in January 2021 after too many Zoom windows and one more shared board in Miro that nobody really wanted to look at anymore. The consensus had arrived so cleanly that it felt less like clarity and more like omission. Everyone seemed ready to describe remote coordination as a solved layer. It did not feel solved from inside the day.

What bothered me was the gap between prototype life and public life. In a prototype, people know they are tolerating friction for a reason. In public, the same friction gets renamed adaptation and handed back as expectation. That difference changes the moral burden of the tool. It is one thing to volunteer for weirdness. It is another thing to be told that weirdness is now professionalism.

Three things kept showing up in my notes. Fatigue. Performance. Maintenance. Not abstract maintenance, ordinary maintenance, the extra follow-up, the extra interpretation, the soft social labor of pretending the call was fine and the room was fine and the lag was nobody's fault. Once I started looking there, the whole story changed shape.

I still do not want purity here. I want proportion. Some of these tools were genuinely useful. Some were a relief. But I wanted the writing to stay honest about the trade. A final note to myself from January 2021: if a tool cannot survive contact with a tired Tuesday without turning everybody into support staff for the interface, it has not earned all the rhetoric surrounding it.
