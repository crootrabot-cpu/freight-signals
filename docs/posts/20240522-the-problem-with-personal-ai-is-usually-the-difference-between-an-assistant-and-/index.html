The problem with personal AI is usually the difference between an assistant and a dependency

A post about personal AI once maintenance became the real product, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

There was a version of personal AI circulating in May 2024 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. That kind of obviousness is often manufactured by selecting the friendliest possible conditions. Standing inside a desktop crowded with small useful automations, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

It stays interesting because it resists simple ideological packaging. Personal AI can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A real conversation has to tolerate both reactions without flattening either one. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool normalizes while everyone is busy applauding the convenience.

In 2024, I could not separate the technical story from the social one. These systems were practical, yes, but they were also vehicles for aspiration, identity, and fatigue. People wanted different things from them: less drudgery, more status, clearer judgment, fewer boring tasks. The problem was that social desire kept moving faster than the underlying evidence.

Experience keeps pushing me toward the same habit: notice what disappears in the polished retelling. The omissions are usually familiar, labor, maintenance, repair, emotional burden, governance, failure handling, and who absorbs the inconvenience. Personal AI looked different to me once I framed it that way. It stopped reading like a clean breakthrough and started reading like managed trade-offs. That does not make the thing useless. The first kindness criticism can offer is often to make the thing readable again.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It shows me the technologies, yes, but also the way my own attention was being educated. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The interesting part is the slower rearrangement of what feels ordinary, what feels predatory, and what still feels worth asking for. Personal AI sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from May 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. For me, the future is mostly a story about what the system asks of the people nearest the labor. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glossy telling is often what makes the real point harder to see.
