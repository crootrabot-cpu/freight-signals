I trust agents more when they look a little unfinished

A personal note on agents, reliability, and why unfinished-looking systems can be easier to trust.

I wrote this down in September 2025 after thinking about agents from the vantage point of a train ride where the future looked mostly like error handling. I was less interested in the headline claim than in the older question beneath it, what kind of world does this thing presume is already waiting? Technology conversations usually lose that part first. Most discussions rush from capability to performance to scale and leave the question of livability trailing behind. Agents seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The mechanism that mattered to me was not the obvious one from the demo version of the story. Public language tends to grab the most visible element first: the model, the dashboard, the robot, the bit that photographs well. The part that becomes socially important is usually the second-order shift around the technology. They change pacing. They redraw the line between the people allowed to remain flexible and the people required to become legible to the system. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, agents stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the older question of atmosphere. Some tools widen the room around a person. Others make every surface feel like an input field. That distinction does not show up neatly in a benchmark, but it shapes daily life anyway. When a system leaves people feeling watched, hurried, or permission-bound, that is not just collateral. It is part of what the system is making normal.

Looking back from September 2025, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “The problem with interface trust is usually the strange politics of delegating judgment,” from August 2025. At that point I was still describing what looked appealing on the surface. What held me here was the post-demo layer: upkeep, political assumptions, quiet social sorting, and the emotional terms on which people were being asked to cooperate with the system. I cannot quite call that earlier optimism wrong. It was an optimism with too little contact behind it, not a bad instinct so much as a premature one.

I do not think becoming anti-technology for self-protection solves the real problem. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can like a tool and still ask what sort of world it is slowly arranging around itself. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The better habit is to keep the frame modest until the evidence actually asks for something bigger. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On open weights as the internet learned a new kind of performance.”

A final note to myself from September 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. These days I care less about winning the prediction game than about noticing what a tool asks of the people standing closest to it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The staged version often pushes the real point just out of frame.
