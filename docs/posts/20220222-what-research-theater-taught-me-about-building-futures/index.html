What research theater taught me about building futures

A reflection on research theater and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

There was a version of research theater circulating in February 2022 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. It is usually a story told after somebody has quietly selected the conditions that make the trend look obvious. Standing inside a browser window full of generated images, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

The topic stays worth returning to because it behaves badly inside simple ideological boxes. Research theater can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. The mature version of this argument has to make room for both sides of that feeling. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I take it to mean asking what the tool is teaching about compliance, attention, and scale while people celebrate its elegance.

In 2022, the social meaning of these systems mattered every bit as much as their mechanics. People adopted them for practical reasons, but also to rehearse a version of themselves: early, thoughtful, efficient, future-facing, serious. I sympathized with that impulse. Still, once identity gets tangled up with adoption, the shared story tends to outrun the quieter reality on the ground.

Over time I have learned to watch what disappears once a story starts getting repeated professionally. Most of the absent material lives in the same neighborhood: labor, maintenance, emotional cost, repair, governance, and asymmetric inconvenience. Research theater looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had first appeared smooth began to show its seams. That does not make the thing useless. The earliest service criticism tends to provide is clarity of shape.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It lets me track not only the movement of the technologies but the movement of my own noticing. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What stays interesting to me is the slower drift in what starts to feel normal, what starts to feel extractive, and what still seems worth wanting. Research theater sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from February 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What holds my attention now is not the prediction itself but the burden a tool shifts onto the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
