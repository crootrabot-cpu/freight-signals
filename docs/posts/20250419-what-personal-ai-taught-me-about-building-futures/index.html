Personal AI only works if it stays personal

A personal essay about why personal AI only works when it resists flattening the person it claims to help.

I wrote this down in April 2025 after thinking about personal AI from the vantage point of a train ride where the future looked mostly like error handling. I kept drifting past the headline toward the quieter question underneath it: what kind of world was this idea written for? That is often the first thing technology makes harder to see. The conversation tends to favor capability over context, performance over maintenance, and scale over the simple question of livability. Personal AI seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The public version kept pointing at one mechanism, but it was another one that felt socially decisive to me. When people talk in public they reach first for the visible layer, the thing that demos cleanly and fits in a headline. What makes most tools matter is less the demo than the secondary effects around it. They change pacing. They quietly decide who may stay general and who must become machine-readable. They change what falls through the cracks because the cracks themselves move. Once you notice that shift, personal AI stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The atmosphere question persists for me because metrics are bad at noticing it. One system leaves room for human judgment. Another makes the whole environment feel permissioned, prompted, and monitored. The difference may be tough to measure, but it is easy to live inside. A tool’s atmosphere is part of its result, not just a backdrop.

Looking back from April 2025, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “On interface trust after the magic show,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In the earlier piece, I was still writing mainly about what first looked attractive. I found myself looking past the applause toward the maintenance burden, the politics, and the quieter emotional contract being written underneath the feature set. It would be too simple to call that earlier optimism wrong. It was incomplete in the familiar way optimism becomes incomplete before the machinery has touched enough real lives.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can like a tool and still ask what sort of world it is slowly arranging around itself. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version usually starts by distrusting the oversized story until the smaller one has earned the right to expand. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “I keep coming back to synthetic voice.”

A final note to myself from April 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. These days I care less about winning the prediction game than about noticing what a tool asks of the people standing closest to it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. Flashiness tends to redirect attention away from what matters.
