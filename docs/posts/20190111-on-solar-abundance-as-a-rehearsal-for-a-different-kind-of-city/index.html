On solar abundance as a rehearsal for a different kind of city

A post about solar abundance as a rehearsal for a different kind of city, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

My notes on solar abundance began with a small irritation in January 2019. The story had become common sense so quickly that the consensus itself started to look suspicious. The quicker the consensus, the more likely it is that someone simplified the story past the point of honesty. In this case the omitted piece was what happens when a dream survives only as branding. At ground level, the question shows up through labor, error burden, trust sequencing, and the people who benefit from the arrangement.

Solar abundance appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The mechanical side is easier to render in public because it turns quickly into components, dashboards, and demonstrations. The fantasy side is less convenient to discuss, though it matters no less. A tool is never just a function, it also carries a social sketch of who counts as the right user. You can often hear the assumptions plainly once you stop looking only at the headline features. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the pattern of reliance the system builds into ordinary life.

What mattered most was the distance between prototype logic and ordinary public life. Prototype conditions at least make the experimental nature of the work explicit. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Prototype enthusiasm changes the moral shape of a test in ways people like to ignore. It is one thing among volunteers and another inside systems people cannot casually opt out of. What happens when a dream survives only as branding becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

The topic still held me, but my patience for abstract phrasing did not. After writing “A note on human-scale robotics when everything still felt handmade,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim about the future cannot survive contact with somebody's day, their tools, their fatigue, and their incentives, I do not expect it to last. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

The thing I keep wanting is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. What matters is noticing when usefulness starts hardening into dependence and weirdness gets sold back to us as fate. Solar abundance seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. More often the shift is relational before it is technological, and it becomes normal before it becomes examined.

A final note to myself from January 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point the burden a tool places on nearby people matters more to me than the abstract future it points toward. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. Flashiness often works by pulling the eye away from the important part.
