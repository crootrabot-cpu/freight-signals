What scaling laws actually predict, and where they quietly stop

This breaks down what scaling laws reliably forecast, their unstated limits, and where nearly everyone misapplies them.

Main notes

  1. 01

    They predict loss will drop smoothly with compute, not that any specific capability will hit a given threshold.

  2. 02

    No original scaling law paper ever claimed general reasoning would emerge. That was added later by commentators.

  3. 03

    The curves only hold when you do not change architecture, training data or training objective.

  4. 04

    Past a certain point, scaling stops improving factual accuracy even while training loss keeps falling.

  5. 05

    I have never seen a public prediction that correctly mapped scaling spend to a real world capability.

Further thoughts

  • Almost every scaling argument online uses the graph, but never reads the original paper's caveats.
  • These are empirical observations, not fundamental unbreakable laws of physics.
  • Nobody actually knows if the curve holds for the next order of magnitude of compute.
  • The quietest failure point is that lower loss does not equal more trustworthy output.
