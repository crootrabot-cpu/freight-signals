A note on mutual aid apps after convenience turned into dependency

A rougher note from February 2020 on mutual aid apps, coordination, and the world implied by convenience software.

I wrote this down in February 2020 after a late-night tab spiral full of neighborhood spreadsheets, delivery screenshots, and the uneasy feeling that convenience software was quietly becoming social infrastructure. Mutual aid apps interested me, but not for the reasons people put in the headline. The question underneath was simpler. What kind of life does this tool assume is already in place?

The public story usually starts with the obvious layer, the app, the dashboard, the signup flow, the thing you can screenshot. The social story starts a layer lower. Who has to stay available. Who has to become legible. Who ends up doing the quiet repair work when the software runs out and the need does not. That was the part I kept circling.

This post is shorter than it should be because I was still figuring out what bothered me. I did not yet have a clean theory. I only had a sensation that some systems arrive offering help and then slowly retrain everyone around them into dependency, measurement, and queue management. That may still be too broad, but it felt true enough to write down.

A final note to myself from February 2020: the future matters to me less as prediction than as a map of what the system demands up close. Admiration is cheap. The better question is whether the tool leaves people with more room to improvise together once the dashboard closes.
