The problem with benchmarks is usually how novelty can impersonate depth

A personal essay about benchmarks, and the longer argument hidden inside how novelty can impersonate depth.

There was a version of benchmarks circulating in June 2022 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. The trend tends to look obvious only after somebody curates the scene around it. Standing inside a group chat melting down over the same screenshot, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

The topic stays worth returning to because it behaves badly inside simple ideological boxes. Benchmarks can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A real conversation has to tolerate both reactions without flattening either one. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I take it to mean asking what the tool is teaching about compliance, attention, and scale while people celebrate its elegance.

By 2022, it was obvious that the social layer mattered as much as the technical one. These systems were not only tools, they were ways of performing judgment, modernity, competence, and sometimes hope. People wanted relief, status, clarity, momentum, or just a way out of dull work. That is understandable. The danger is that identity and evidence start moving at different speeds, and institutions often side with identity first.

The longer I do this, the more I watch the details that fall away once the polished retelling begins. Most professional retellings drop the same things, labor, governance, repair, emotional cost, and who pays when the system misbehaves. Benchmarks looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had seemed seamless began to look more like trade-offs with good styling. That does not make the thing useless. Legibility is often the first service criticism performs well.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It turns the archive into a record of the technologies and of the eye I was bringing to them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What stays interesting to me is the slower drift in what starts to feel normal, what starts to feel extractive, and what still seems worth wanting. Benchmarks sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from June 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What stays in focus for me now is not the headline forecast but the burden a tool pushes onto ordinary people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
