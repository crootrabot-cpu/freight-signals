On safety debates as the internet learned a new kind of performance

A post about safety debates as the internet learned a new kind of performance, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

I wrote this down in January 2022 after thinking about safety debates from the vantage point of a quiet hour reading papers with too many grand nouns. What held me was not the headline but the deeper question underneath it: what sort of world is this idea written for? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. The usual sequence favors capability over context, performance over upkeep, and scale over livability. Safety debates seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. Public explanations tend to favor the most visible element, the piece that fits in a headline or a screenshot. Most tools matter less because of the demo and more because of the secondary effects they set loose. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a person with context and who must become a neat input. They change what falls through the cracks because the cracks themselves move. Once you notice that shift, safety debates stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is a version of this argument that ignores atmosphere, and I do not trust it. Some systems open up breathing room. Others make every action feel slightly supervised. Measurement rarely captures the difference cleanly, but daily life does. When a tool thickens procedure and shrinks the room people feel around themselves, that belongs in the evaluation.

Looking back from January 2022, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “What telepresence rituals taught me about building futures,” from October 2021. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. The interesting part, for me, had moved past the applause and into maintenance, governance, social sorting, and the emotional deal the system expected people to accept. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. The problem was not optimism itself but how little reality it had yet encountered.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Useful tools should invite scrutiny too, especially about the world they normalize. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The healthy move is to keep the story small until reality proves it deserves to grow. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “What distributed energy taught me about building futures.”

A final note to myself from January 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I read the future mainly through the demands a tool places on its closest users. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
