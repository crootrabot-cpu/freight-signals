On digital twins while everyone tried to make software feel responsible

A reflection on digital twins and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

My notes on digital twins began with a small irritation in October 2024. The consensus had arrived so cleanly that it felt less like clarity and more like omission. When consensus arrives that quickly, I assume some important rough edge has been sanded off to make it travel better. In this case the omitted piece was the strange politics of delegating judgment. What looks abstract in prose becomes very specific in life: upkeep, harm absorption, trust order, and who gets to market that order as progress.

Digital twins appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. Talking about the engineering is straightforward because it collapses cleanly into diagrams, benchmarks, and staged proof. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. Every tool drags along a picture of its preferred user and a social setting it would like to stabilize. You can often hear the assumptions plainly once you stop looking only at the headline features. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the emotional and behavioral terms on which the system asks to be accepted.

I kept coming back to the distance between prototype life and public life. The prototype has one advantage, nobody mistakes it for settled reality. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. A tool behaves very differently when it is tried first on people who opted in for the strange version. It is another thing to build the same logic into institutions and hope design language can hide the cost. The strange politics of delegating judgment becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

The topic still held me, but my patience for abstract phrasing did not. After writing “The problem with memory systems is usually the difference between an assistant and a dependency,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. Once a future-facing claim fails contact with ordinary life, I assume the problem is with the claim rather than the day. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. What matters is noticing when usefulness starts hardening into dependence and weirdness gets sold back to us as fate. Digital twins seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Most of the time it becomes real first as a relationship style, not as a cleanly named invention.

A final note to myself from October 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What I keep watching now is not the prediction but the pressure a tool transfers onto nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
