The problem with coordination systems is usually software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit

A reflection on coordination systems and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in November 2021 after thinking about coordination systems from the vantage point of the unnerving quiet of a masked grocery line. What held me was not the headline but the deeper question underneath it: what sort of world is this idea written for? That is often the first thing technology makes harder to see. Most conversations rush from capability to performance to scale while the livability question trails behind. Coordination systems seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The socially important mechanism was not the one public explanations liked to point at first. The public version usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, robot, control surface. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a generalist and who has to become legible to the machine. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, coordination systems stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is a version of this argument that ignores atmosphere, and I do not trust it. Some systems open up breathing room. Others make every action feel slightly supervised. Measurement rarely captures the difference cleanly, but daily life does. When a tool thickens procedure and shrinks the room people feel around themselves, that belongs in the evaluation.

Looking back from November 2021, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “What telepresence rituals taught me about building futures,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. That note was still working at the level of first attraction. The real subject had shifted for me from the performance to the obligations that trailed behind it. The earlier optimism was not exactly wrong to my mind. It was the sort of incomplete optimism you get before the machinery has had time to reach enough real lives.

I do not see withdrawal from technology as the right lesson here. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. A good tool still deserves questions about the world it makes easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by resisting the big story until the smaller one proves itself durable. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On maker cities as a rehearsal for a different kind of city.”

A final note to myself from November 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I worry less about the winner of the argument now than about the people asked to take the system into daily life. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The flashy rendering is often where attention slips off the important part.
