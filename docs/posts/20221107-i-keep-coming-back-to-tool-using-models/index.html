I keep coming back to tool-using models

An essay on tool-using models, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

My notes on tool-using models began with a small irritation in November 2022. I was struck by how quickly the larger story hardened into common sense, and that hardening made me suspicious. When a formulation travels instantly, I assume some awkward but important detail got left behind. In this case the omitted piece was the difference between surprise and understanding. What looks abstract in prose becomes very specific in life: upkeep, harm absorption, trust order, and who gets to market that order as progress.

Tool-using models appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The technical side tends to narrate itself through parts, systems, metrics, and a convenient demo culture. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. What arrives with a tool is not just capability but an implied person and an implied arrangement of life. If you pay attention, those assumptions are right there in the design. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the behavioral atmosphere the system smuggles in under the banner of functionality.

I kept coming back to the distance between prototype life and public life. Inside a prototype, everyone knows they are participating in a test. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. There is a big difference between testing on willing enthusiasts and asking ordinary people to inherit the result. It is different once the same logic enters public life and people are asked to call it progress because the interface looks clean. The difference between surprise and understanding becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

My taste for the subject stayed mostly intact, but my threshold for abstraction fell sharply. After writing “A note on chat interfaces before the story settled,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim about the future cannot survive contact with somebody's day, their tools, their fatigue, and their incentives, I do not expect it to last. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. What matters is noticing when usefulness starts hardening into dependence and weirdness gets sold back to us as fate. Tool-using models seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually it arrives as a relationship pattern that turns ordinary before anyone has really consented to it.

A final note to myself from November 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I mostly use the future now as a lens for seeing what the system asks of nearby workers. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
