I keep coming back to personal AI

A post about personal AI as the tools got more useful and less theatrical, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

There was a version of personal AI circulating in October 2024 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. That kind of obviousness is often manufactured by selecting the friendliest possible conditions. Standing inside an office where the exciting part was no longer the model but the glue, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep circling back to it because it refuses clean ideological categories. Personal AI can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Any serious version of the conversation has to hold both reactions at once. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I take it to mean asking what the tool is teaching about compliance, attention, and scale while people celebrate its elegance.

In 2024, I could not separate the technical story from the social one. These systems were practical, yes, but they were also vehicles for aspiration, identity, and fatigue. People wanted different things from them: less drudgery, more status, clearer judgment, fewer boring tasks. The problem was that social desire kept moving faster than the underlying evidence.

The longer I do this, the more I watch the details that fall away once the polished retelling begins. Usually the missing pieces come from the same family: labor, error handling, emotional load, repair, governance, and unevenly distributed inconvenience. Personal AI looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had first appeared smooth began to show its seams. That does not make the thing useless. Sometimes criticism is valuable simply because it makes the object legible again.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It shows me the technologies, yes, but also the way my own attention was being educated. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The lasting question is how the slower shift changes what feels ordinary, parasitic, or still worth wanting. Personal AI sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from October 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What holds me less is the argument about what comes next than the pressure a tool exerts on nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The prettier version is often the one that helps us miss the important thing.
