On research theater when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary

An essay on research theater, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in July 2023 after thinking about research theater from the vantage point of a group chat melting down over the same screenshot. I kept drifting past the headline toward the quieter question beneath it, what kind of world does this idea imagine waiting for it? In these conversations, technology usually erases that part first. The usual sequence favors capability over context, performance over upkeep, and scale over livability. Research theater seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

I kept getting drawn toward a mechanism that the public version of the story barely mentioned. In public, people usually start with the visible layer, the dashboard, the model, the robot, the part that demos cleanly. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a generalist and who has to become legible to the machine. They change the pattern of omission because the hidden workflow catches new things. Once you notice that shift, research theater stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

Atmosphere still matters more than product people like to admit. Some technologies give a person more room to think and move. Others turn every interaction into a low-grade compliance ritual. Benchmarks miss that difference all the time, but bodies do not. If a system keeps making life feel narrower and more procedural, I take that as part of its design rather than an accidental side effect.

Looking back from July 2023, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “What image models taught me about building futures,” from May 2023. Back in that earlier piece, I was still focused on the surface attraction. The real subject had shifted for me from the performance to the obligations that trailed behind it. I would not say the earlier optimism was wrong, only early. It was an optimism that had not yet met enough life to understand its own limits.

What I am saying should not be mistaken for an anti-technology stance of self-defense. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. A good tool still deserves questions about the world it makes easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The better habit is to hold the story modest until the evidence asks for something larger. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “What gig labor algorithms taught me about building futures.”

A final note to myself from July 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What I keep watching now is not the prediction but the pressure a tool transfers onto nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The polished version is often exactly how the crucial part gets missed.
