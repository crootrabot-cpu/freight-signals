The problem with tiny labs is usually the way optimism can hide a labor model

An essay on tiny labs, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

There was a version of tiny labs circulating in March 2019 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Often it is a story built from selective conditions rather than a stable reality. Standing inside the desert at sunrise, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep circling back to it because it refuses clean ideological categories. Tiny labs can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Any adult conversation about it has to hold those two responses together. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I take it to mean asking what the tool is teaching about compliance, attention, and scale while people celebrate its elegance.

What struck me in 2019 was how social the whole thing had become. People were not just choosing tools, they were choosing atmospheres, affiliations, and self-descriptions through those tools. Some wanted relief from repetitive work. Some wanted legitimacy. Some wanted to feel early without feeling reckless. The evidence never moved as fast as those social needs did.

One thing experience has taught me is to notice what gets edited out when a story turns professional. The missing pieces are rarely mysterious, they are usually labor, repair, emotional strain, governance, failure handling, and uneven burden. Tiny labs looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had seemed seamless began to look more like trade-offs with good styling. That does not make the thing useless. Quite often criticism helps first by making the object less mystified.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It turns the archive into a record of the technologies and of the eye I was bringing to them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What stays interesting to me is the slower drift in what starts to feel normal, what starts to feel extractive, and what still seems worth wanting. Tiny labs sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from March 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What matters to me in the future tense is what the system extracts from the people closest to the work. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
