Slow writing is one way to stay visible to yourself

A note on slow writing as a defense against ambient convenience and flattened thought.

I wrote this down in April 2026 after thinking about slow writing from the vantage point of a small desk in good light. I was less interested in the headline claim than in the older question beneath it, what kind of world does this thing presume is already waiting? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. The conversation tends to favor capability over context, performance over maintenance, and scale over the simple question of livability. Slow writing seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The mechanism I cared about was mostly off to the side of the official explanation. The conversation in public usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, dashboard, robot. But most technologies become socially important through a second-order effect. They change pacing. They quietly decide who may stay general and who must become machine-readable. They change what can be ignored because some hidden workflow now catches it. Once you notice that shift, slow writing stops looking like a gadget and starts looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the old problem of atmosphere. Some technologies make people feel more room around themselves. Others make every surface feel like an input. Because it resists tidy measurement, that difference tends to vanish from the official accounting. But mood is one of the outputs. That kind of narrowing effect is part of what the system is, even if nobody wanted to advertise it. It is part of what the product is. Arguments that dismiss human texture as softness next to performance have never done much for me.

Looking back from April 2026, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “What trustworthy interfaces taught me about building futures,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. What held me here was the post-demo layer: upkeep, political assumptions, quiet social sorting, and the emotional terms on which people were being asked to cooperate with the system. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was an optimism with too little contact behind it, not a bad instinct so much as a premature one.

I do not think the goal is to become anti-technology out of self-defense. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Good tools deserve scrutiny too, especially about the kind of world they make easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version usually starts by distrusting the oversized story until the smaller one has earned the right to expand. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “The problem with safety debates is usually how novelty can impersonate depth.”

A final note to myself from April 2026: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. These days I care less about winning the prediction game than about noticing what a tool asks of the people standing closest to it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
