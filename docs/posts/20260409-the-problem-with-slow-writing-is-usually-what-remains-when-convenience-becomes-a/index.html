Slow writing is one way to stay visible to yourself

A note on slow writing as a defense against ambient convenience and flattened thought.

I wrote this down in April 2026 after thinking about slow writing from the vantage point of a small desk in good light. I was less interested in the headline claim than in the older question beneath it, what kind of world does this thing presume is already waiting? With technology, that is usually the first thing to vanish. The conversation tends to favor capability over context, performance over maintenance, and scale over the simple question of livability. Slow writing seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The mechanism I cared about was mostly off to the side of the official explanation. The conversation in public usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, dashboard, robot. The part that becomes socially important is usually the second-order shift around the technology. They change pacing. They quietly decide who may stay general and who must become machine-readable. They change what falls through the cracks because the cracks themselves move. Once you notice that shift, slow writing stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The atmosphere around a tool is not decorative, it is part of the thing being built. Some systems create room for judgment and improvisation. Others turn ordinary life into a corridor of tiny permissions. That shift can be difficult to benchmark, but it is impossible to live inside without noticing. The mood around a system tells you something real about its politics.

Looking back from April 2026, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “What trustworthy interfaces taught me about building futures,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. At that point I was still describing what looked appealing on the surface. What held me here was the post-demo layer: upkeep, political assumptions, quiet social sorting, and the emotional terms on which people were being asked to cooperate with the system. I do not read that earlier optimism as a mistake, exactly. It was an optimism with too little contact behind it, not a bad instinct so much as a premature one.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Good tools deserve scrutiny too, especially about the kind of world they make easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version usually starts by distrusting the oversized story until the smaller one has earned the right to expand. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “The problem with safety debates is usually how novelty can impersonate depth.”

A final note to myself from April 2026: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. These days I care less about winning the prediction game than about noticing what a tool asks of the people standing closest to it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The sleek version is often how the essential part slips past us.
