I keep coming back to fragile supply chains

A reflection on fragile supply chains and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

There was a version of fragile supply chains circulating in May 2021 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Often it is a story built from selective conditions rather than a stable reality. Standing inside a video call where everyone looked slightly ghosted by their own webcam, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

It remains interesting partly because it resists being filed into one neat ideological drawer. Fragile supply chains can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. The mature version of this argument has to make room for both sides of that feeling. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool normalizes while everyone is busy applauding the convenience.

What struck me in 2021 was how social the whole thing had become. The tools carried atmospheres and affiliations with them, and people were choosing those too. Some wanted relief from repetitive work. Some wanted legitimacy. Some wanted to feel early without feeling reckless. Evidence could not keep up with the speed of those social needs.

I have learned to study the omissions that arrive with the professional version of a story. Usually the missing pieces come from the same family: labor, error handling, emotional load, repair, governance, and unevenly distributed inconvenience. Fragile supply chains looked different to me once I framed it that way. The sleek solution slowly resolved into a stack of trade-offs wearing persuasive language. That does not make the thing useless. Before criticism does anything grander, it often just restores readability.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It helps me see what the technologies were doing and what kind of observer they were turning me into. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The enduring question, to me, is the slower change in what feels ordinary, what feels parasitic, and what still feels worth wanting. Fragile supply chains sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from May 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. These days I read the burden more closely than the forecast. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous framing often puts the important part out of easy reach.
