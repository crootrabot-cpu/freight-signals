The problem with fragile supply chains is usually the difference between resilience and improvisation

A post about fragile supply chains while daily life was being rerouted through software, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

My notes on fragile supply chains began with a small irritation in January 2020. Everyone around me seemed strangely satisfied with the headline version, which only made me more suspicious. When a formulation travels instantly, I assume some awkward but important detail got left behind. In this case the omitted piece was the difference between resilience and improvisation. From a distance it sounds conceptual, but nearby it turns into a question about who keeps the system afloat and who pays for its mistakes.

Fragile supply chains appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The engineering story is easy to tell because it arrives with visible pieces and measurable claims. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. Tools show up carrying assumptions about who will use them well and what kind of world should make that use feel natural. The design tends to confess those assumptions to anyone willing to look closely. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the kinds of behavior, dependency, and mood the system quietly teaches while it works.

The real split, to my eye, was between prototype logic and public-life conditions. One virtue of prototype life is that everyone knows the terms are still experimental. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. It is one thing to test a tool among enthusiasts who signed up for weirdness. It is another thing to build the same logic into institutions and hope design language can hide the cost. The difference between resilience and improvisation becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

The real shift was not in my taste for the subject but in how little vague language I was willing to tolerate. After writing “What communal tools taught me about building futures,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. Once a future-facing claim fails contact with ordinary life, I assume the problem is with the claim rather than the day. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The part worth watching is the moment a helpful system begins to act like a dependency. Fragile supply chains seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. More often the shift is relational before it is technological, and it becomes normal before it becomes examined.

A final note to myself from January 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What matters more to me now is who has to carry the system day by day, not who wins the debate around it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
