A note on agents when reliability turned out to be the whole game

A personal essay about agents, and the longer argument hidden inside the long argument between autonomy and auditability.

There was a version of agents circulating in February 2024 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Most inevitable-sounding stories become inevitable only after the setup has been carefully narrowed. Standing inside a train ride where the future looked mostly like error handling, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

Part of what keeps this topic alive is that it refuses tidy ideological sorting. Agents can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A grown-up conversation has to hold both truths at once. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I read the question as one about what kinds of compliance and attention the tool normalizes under the cover of ease.

By 2024, the systems were doing double duty as tools and as social signals. Part of the draw was practical relief. Part of it was the flattering image of the person using them. That is where my caution kicked in. When a system starts lending people identity, the claims around it tend to outrun the evidence.

Experience keeps pushing me toward the same habit: notice what disappears in the polished retelling. Most of the absent material lives in the same neighborhood: labor, maintenance, emotional cost, repair, governance, and asymmetric inconvenience. Agents looked different to me once I framed it that way. Over time the sleekness gave way to the more familiar shape of trade-offs dressed for release. That does not make the thing useless. A lot of criticism is useful first because it restores readability.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It shows me both the technologies and the slow education of my own attention. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The interesting part is the slower rearrangement of what feels ordinary, what feels predatory, and what still feels worth asking for. Agents sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from February 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I pay attention to the future mainly as a way of seeing what the system requires of its nearest operators. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The shiny version is often the easiest way to overlook what actually matters.
