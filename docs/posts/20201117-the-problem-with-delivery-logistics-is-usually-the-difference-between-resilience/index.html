The problem with delivery logistics is usually the difference between resilience and improvisation

An essay on delivery logistics, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in November 2020 after thinking about delivery logistics from the vantage point of an empty downtown street. The headline claim interested me less than the older question below it: what sort of world is this idea already presuming? That is often the first thing technology makes harder to see. We keep prioritizing capability, performance, and scale while deferring the question of whether the system improves daily life. Delivery logistics seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The mechanism I cared about sat slightly to the side of the official explanation. The public version usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, robot, control surface. What makes a technology socially consequential is often the second-order effect, not the headline capability. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a person with context and who must become a neat input. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, delivery logistics stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

Atmosphere matters to me precisely because metrics tend to underread it. One system leaves room for human judgment. Another turns the place into a series of nudges, checks, and minor permissions. It may be difficult to quantify, though it is easy enough to feel. A tool’s output includes the mood it creates around ordinary life.

Looking back from November 2020, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “A note on public health dashboards after convenience turned into dependency,” from July 2020. Back in that earlier piece, I was still focused on the surface attraction. The interesting part, for me, had moved past the applause and into maintenance, governance, social sorting, and the emotional deal the system expected people to accept. I would not say the earlier optimism was wrong, only early. The optimism mostly lacked contact with enough real lives to know where its edges were.

I am not proposing anti-technology as a defensive posture. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Useful tools should invite scrutiny too, especially about the world they normalize. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by resisting the big story until the smaller one proves itself durable. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On domestic robots as a rehearsal for a different kind of city.”

A final note to myself from November 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What matters to me in the future tense is what the system extracts from the people closest to the work. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The staged version often pushes the real point just out of frame.
