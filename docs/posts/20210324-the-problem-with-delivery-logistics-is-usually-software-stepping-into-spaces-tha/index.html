The problem with delivery logistics is usually software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit

A personal essay about delivery logistics, and the longer argument hidden inside software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit.

My notes on delivery logistics began with a small irritation in March 2021. The story had become common sense so quickly that the consensus itself started to look suspicious. A sentence that lands too cleanly usually owes its smoothness to missing context. In this case the omitted piece was software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit. The abstract version vanishes as soon as you track who has to maintain the thing, who bears its failures, and who gains from the trust it extracts.

Delivery logistics appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. Engineering is easier to discuss because it looks concrete almost immediately. The imagined side is harder to formalize, but it matters no less. The tool arrives with an image of its user already drawn in, along with a preferred backdrop for that user. The design is already telling you about those assumptions if you let it. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the style of dependence the system teaches while everyone is busy calling it useful.

Again and again I ran into the difference between a prototype setting and a public one. In a prototype, people understand that the thing is provisional. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. A tool behaves very differently when it is tried first on people who opted in for the strange version. It is another thing entirely to fold that same logic into schools, offices, hospitals, transit, or housing and expect gratitude because the surface looks modern. Software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

The real shift was not in my taste for the subject but in how little vague language I was willing to tolerate. After writing “On telepresence rituals under pressure,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim cannot withstand the texture of a real day, it does not earn much staying power in my mind. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

The goal, for me, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. But we should stay attentive to the conditions under which usefulness turns into dependence and novelty gets marketed as destiny. Delivery logistics seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. What becomes durable is often less the tool than the pattern of relationship it makes routine.

A final note to myself from March 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The debate about the future holds me less than the pressure a tool puts on its nearest neighbors. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous framing is usually what keeps the important part out of view.
