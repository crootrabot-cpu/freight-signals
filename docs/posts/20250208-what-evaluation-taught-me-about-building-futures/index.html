Evaluation gets harder right when it starts to matter

A post about evaluation and the uneasy fact that measurement gets harder exactly when people start depending on it.

My notes on evaluation began with a small irritation in February 2025. The speed of the agreement bothered me more than the claim itself. When a consensus sounds too polished, I start asking what was trimmed away to make it travel so well. In this case the omitted piece was the difference between an assistant and a dependency. On the ground it becomes a practical matter of upkeep, error absorption, social risk, and who gets rewarded for calling all of that progress.

Evaluation appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The engineering half is always easier to narrate because it collapses neatly into components, metrics, and demos. The imagined side is harder to formalize, but it matters no less. Tools are never just functions, they come with built-in assumptions about the user and the world around that user. The design choices usually tell on those assumptions if you read them closely. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the kinds of behavior and dependency the system teaches people to accept.

I kept noticing the moral distance between a prototype and the public environment it eventually enters. In a prototype, people understand that the thing is provisional. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Prototype enthusiasm changes the moral shape of a test in ways people like to ignore. It is one thing in a prototype and another in schools, transit, hospitals, housing, and workplaces where people inherit the system as environment. The difference between an assistant and a dependency becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

The subject still interested me, but my threshold for abstraction dropped hard. After writing “A note on local inference when reliability turned out to be the whole game,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. Once a grand claim fails contact with an ordinary day, I assume the problem is with the claim rather than the day. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

The right target here is proportion, not purity. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. What matters is noticing the point where a useful thing becomes something you are no longer really free to decline. Evaluation seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually the deeper change is a social relationship that hardens into routine before anyone names it clearly.

A final note to myself from February 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The part I keep returning to is not the prediction but the burden placed on the people expected to absorb the tool into daily life. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The prettier version is often the one that helps us miss the important thing.
