I keep coming back to local inference

A post about local inference as the tools got more useful and less theatrical, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

My notes on local inference began with a small irritation in February 2024. The speed of the agreement bothered me before the content of the agreement did. A clean sentence can be useful, but when it becomes too clean I start looking for the missing friction. In this case the omitted piece was what happens when a product grows more competent than legible. At ground level, the question shows up through labor, error burden, trust sequencing, and the people who benefit from the arrangement.

Local inference appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The technical half is easier to narrate because it already comes with numbers, components, and something you can point at. The fantasy layer is harder to talk about, but it matters every bit as much. Each tool contains a quiet model of the user and the kind of society that should feel normal around it. The design choices usually tell on those assumptions if you read them closely. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about what kind of daily conduct the system quietly asks people to adopt.

Again and again I ran into the difference between a prototype setting and a public one. Prototype life is honest in one important way, the terms still announce themselves as experimental. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. The first audience is often self-selected for novelty, which makes the test environment gentler than public life. It is another matter to embed that logic in ordinary institutions and expect people to thank you for polishing the surface. What happens when a product grows more competent than legible becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

What changed most was not my interest in the topic but my tolerance for abstraction. After writing “A note on image models while the hype still outran the evidence,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. Any claim about the future that collapses on contact with ordinary life does not earn much trust from me. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

Purity is not the point for me. Proportion is. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The key thing is to watch for the point where a useful thing stops feeling optional. Local inference seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. More often it is a style of relationship that becomes normal before we have finished deciding whether we want it.

A final note to myself from February 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point the burden a tool places on nearby people matters more to me than the abstract future it points toward. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The sleek version is often how the essential part slips past us.
