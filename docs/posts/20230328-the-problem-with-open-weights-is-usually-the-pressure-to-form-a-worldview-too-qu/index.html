The problem with open weights is usually the pressure to form a worldview too quickly

An essay on open weights, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

There was a version of open weights circulating in March 2023 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Most inevitable-sounding stories become inevitable only after the setup has been carefully narrowed. Standing inside an exhausted midnight walk after too much time online, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I return to it because it misbehaves inside the usual ideological categories. Open weights can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. The mature version of this argument has to make room for both sides of that feeling. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I take it to mean asking what the tool is teaching about compliance, attention, and scale while people celebrate its elegance.

In 2023, I could not separate the technical story from the social one. These systems were practical, yes, but they were also vehicles for aspiration, identity, and fatigue. People wanted different things from them: less drudgery, more status, clearer judgment, fewer boring tasks. The problem was that social desire kept moving faster than the underlying evidence.

Over time I have learned to watch what disappears once a story starts getting repeated professionally. Most professional retellings drop the same things, labor, governance, repair, emotional cost, and who pays when the system misbehaves. Open weights looked different to me once I framed it that way. It stopped reading like a clean breakthrough and started reading like managed trade-offs. That does not make the thing useless. Often the first honest service criticism offers is the return of legibility.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It helps me watch the technologies change and my own standards of attention change with them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The part that lasts is the slower movement in what becomes normal, what becomes extractive, and what continues to feel worth wanting. Open weights sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from March 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point being right about the future matters less than being honest about who carries the burden. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The staged version often pushes the real point just out of frame.
