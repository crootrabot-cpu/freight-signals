On domestic robots as a rehearsal for a different kind of city

A reflection on domestic robots and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

There was a version of domestic robots circulating in June 2019 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Most inevitable-sounding stories become inevitable only after the setup has been carefully narrowed. Standing inside an overcaffeinated dinner where everyone had a sketchbook open, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep circling back to it because it refuses clean ideological categories. Domestic robots can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. If the conversation is serious, it has to keep both of those truths on the table. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool normalizes while everyone is busy applauding the convenience.

In 2019, the social meaning of these systems mattered every bit as much as their mechanics. People adopted them for practical reasons, but also to rehearse a version of themselves: early, thoughtful, efficient, future-facing, serious. I sympathized with that impulse. Still, once identity gets tangled up with adoption, the shared story tends to outrun the quieter reality on the ground.

Experience keeps pushing me toward the same habit: notice what disappears in the polished retelling. Most of the absent material lives in the same neighborhood: labor, maintenance, emotional cost, repair, governance, and asymmetric inconvenience. Domestic robots looked different to me once I framed it that way. It stopped reading like a clean breakthrough and started reading like managed trade-offs. That does not make the thing useless. A lot of criticism is useful first because it restores readability.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It helps me watch the technologies change and my own standards of attention change with them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What remains interesting is the gradual change in what looks normal, what looks extractive, and what still feels worthy of pursuit. Domestic robots sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from June 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What I keep watching now is not the prediction but the pressure a tool transfers onto nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
