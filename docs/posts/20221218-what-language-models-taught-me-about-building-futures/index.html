What language models taught me about building futures

A post about language models when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

There was a version of language models circulating in December 2022 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. It is usually a story told after somebody has quietly selected the conditions that make the trend look obvious. Standing inside a browser window full of generated images, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep circling back to it because it refuses clean ideological categories. Language models can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. If the conversation is serious, it has to keep both of those truths on the table. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I read the question as one about what kinds of compliance and attention the tool normalizes under the cover of ease.

In 2022, the social meaning of these systems mattered every bit as much as their mechanics. People adopted them for practical reasons, but also to rehearse a version of themselves: early, thoughtful, efficient, future-facing, serious. I sympathized with that impulse. Still, once identity gets tangled up with adoption, the shared story tends to outrun the quieter reality on the ground.

One thing experience has taught me is to notice what gets edited out when a story turns professional. It is often the same cluster of absences: labor, upkeep, emotional load, repair, governance, and lopsided inconvenience. Language models looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had seemed polished began to reveal the trade-offs hiding underneath the polish. That does not make the thing useless. Legibility is often the first service criticism performs well.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It helps me watch the technologies change and my own standards of attention change with them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The part that lasts is the slower movement in what becomes normal, what becomes extractive, and what continues to feel worth wanting. Language models sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from December 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What matters to me in the future tense is what the system extracts from the people closest to the work. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
