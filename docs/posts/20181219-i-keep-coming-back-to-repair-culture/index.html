I keep coming back to repair culture

A personal essay about repair culture, and the longer argument hidden inside how quickly spectacle outruns stewardship.

There was a version of repair culture circulating in December 2018 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Often it is a story built from selective conditions rather than a stable reality. Standing inside the desert at sunrise, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

It remains interesting partly because it resists being filed into one neat ideological drawer. Repair culture can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. If the conversation is serious, it has to keep both of those truths on the table. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool normalizes while everyone is busy applauding the convenience.

In 2018, the social texture mattered as much to me as the technical substance. People were not just adopting systems, they were using them to signal taste, seriousness, relief, ambition, even exhaustion. Some wanted to look early. Some wanted to look rigorous. Some simply wanted escape from repetitive work. All of that made sense to me. But once a technology becomes an identity stage, the public story around it tends to outrun the evidence supporting it.

The longer I do this, the more I watch the details that fall away once the polished retelling begins. Most of the absent material lives in the same neighborhood: labor, maintenance, emotional cost, repair, governance, and asymmetric inconvenience. Repair culture looked different to me once I framed it that way. What looked clean at first eventually read like trade-offs with a competent publicist. That does not make the thing useless. Legibility is often the first service criticism performs well.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It records not just what the technologies were doing but how my own eye was changing alongside them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The interesting part is the slower rearrangement of what feels ordinary, what feels predatory, and what still feels worth asking for. Repair culture sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from December 2018: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The debate matters less to me than the people who have to metabolize the system in ordinary life. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
