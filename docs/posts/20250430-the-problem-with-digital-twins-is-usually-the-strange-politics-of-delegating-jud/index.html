A digital twin is still a story about who gets to see

An essay on digital twins and the politics of visibility that come bundled with them.

There was a version of digital twins circulating in April 2025 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. That tone of inevitability usually arrives after the setup has been trimmed to flatter the trend. Standing inside an office where the exciting part was no longer the model but the glue, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

The subject stays interesting partly because it resists the usual ideological sorting rituals. Digital twins can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. If the conversation is serious, it has to keep both of those truths on the table. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool trains us to accept while we are busy admiring how effortless it seems.

By 2025, the systems were doing double duty as tools and as social signals. People were reaching for them because they solved problems, but also because they implied something flattering about the user. That is where my caution kicked in. Once people start borrowing identity from a system, the claims around it tend to grow faster than the proof.

I have learned that the professional retelling of a story is usually the one where the omissions matter most. Most of the missing pieces come from the same family: labor, repair, emotional burden, governance, failure handling, and uneven inconvenience. Digital twins looked different to me once I framed it that way. The elegant solution started to look less elegant and more like a pile of trade-offs with excellent styling. That does not make the thing useless. Sometimes criticism is valuable mainly because it makes the thing readable again.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It lets me see not just what the technologies were doing, but what I was gradually training myself to notice. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The interesting part is the slower rearrangement of what feels ordinary, what feels predatory, and what still feels worth asking for. Digital twins sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from April 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. These days I care less about winning the prediction game than about noticing what a tool asks of the people standing closest to it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
