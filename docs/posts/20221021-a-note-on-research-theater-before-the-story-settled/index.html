A note on research theater before the story settled

A reflection on research theater and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

There was a version of research theater circulating in October 2022 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Often it is a story built from selective conditions rather than a stable reality. Standing inside a coffee shop conversation that turned into an accidental seminar, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

The topic stays worth returning to because it behaves badly inside simple ideological boxes. Research theater can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A grown-up conversation has to hold both truths at once. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. To me it means asking what habits of obedience and attention the tool installs while we celebrate the convenience.

By 2022, it was obvious that the social layer mattered as much as the technical one. These systems were not only tools, they were ways of performing judgment, modernity, competence, and sometimes hope. People wanted relief, status, clarity, momentum, or just a way out of dull work. That is understandable. The danger is that identity and evidence start moving at different speeds, and institutions often side with identity first.

I have learned to study the omissions that arrive with the professional version of a story. Most professional retellings drop the same things, labor, governance, repair, emotional cost, and who pays when the system misbehaves. Research theater looked different to me once I framed it that way. It stopped reading like a clean breakthrough and started reading like managed trade-offs. That does not make the thing useless. Before criticism does anything grander, it often just restores readability.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It gives me a record of the tools and of the standards by which I was learning to judge them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The part that lasts is the slower movement in what becomes normal, what becomes extractive, and what continues to feel worth wanting. Research theater sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from October 2022: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I do not find the argument about what comes next as gripping as the pressure a tool puts on the people living beside it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
