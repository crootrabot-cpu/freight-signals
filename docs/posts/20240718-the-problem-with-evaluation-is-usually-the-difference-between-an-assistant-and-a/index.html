The problem with evaluation is usually the difference between an assistant and a dependency

A reflection on evaluation and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in July 2024 after thinking about evaluation from the vantage point of a desktop crowded with small useful automations. The surface topic mattered less than the background assumption about the world the idea expects to find. With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. We talk about capability before context, performance before maintenance, and scale before asking whether any of it makes life more livable. Evaluation seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The socially important mechanism was not the one public explanations liked to point at first. In public, the visible part gets all the attention, the robot, the interface, the model, the shiny layer on top. Technologies usually matter socially because of their indirect effects, not just their obvious ones. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain complex and who has to tidy themselves up for the system. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, evaluation stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The atmosphere around a tool is not decorative, it is part of the thing being built. Some systems create room for judgment and improvisation. Others turn ordinary life into a corridor of tiny permissions. That shift can be difficult to benchmark, but it is impossible to live inside without noticing. The mood around a system tells you something real about its politics.

Looking back from July 2024, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “What local inference taught me about building futures,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. This time I cared less about the applause and more about what followed it, maintenance, political assumptions, social sorting, and the emotional terms of participation. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. The optimism mostly lacked contact with enough real lives to know where its edges were.

I am not arguing for an anti-technology posture born of self-defense. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. There is no contradiction between appreciating a tool and interrogating the environment it builds. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version keeps the frame narrow until the world forces it wider. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “What warehouse software taught me about building futures.”

A final note to myself from July 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I mostly use the future now as a lens for seeing what the system asks of nearby workers. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
