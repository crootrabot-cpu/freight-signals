On repair culture before venture language flattened the mood

A post about repair culture before venture language flattened the mood, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

I wrote this down in May 2019 after thinking about repair culture from the vantage point of a warehouse party full of hacked machines. I was less interested in the headline version of the topic than in the tougher question underneath it: what kind of world does this idea think it is entering? That tends to be the first thing technology talk smooths over. The order is almost always capability first, then performance, then scale, and only later the question of livability. Repair culture seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The part that mattered to me was not the mechanism people tended to highlight in public. In public, people usually start with the visible layer, the dashboard, the model, the robot, the part that demos cleanly. Technologies usually matter socially because of their indirect effects, not just their obvious ones. They change pacing. They alter who may stay flexible and who must start performing legibility for the machine. They make different things visible or ignorable because the workflow beneath the surface has shifted. Once you notice that shift, repair culture stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

I do not think atmosphere is a soft concern. It is where many technologies tell the truth about themselves. Some leave people feeling more capable and less managed. Others quietly convert daily life into a set of nudges, checks, and approvals. That effect may not show up in a benchmark chart, but it absolutely shows up in the lived experience of the system.

Looking back from May 2019, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “On solar abundance as a rehearsal for a different kind of city,” from January 2019. In the earlier piece, I was still writing mainly about what first looked attractive. I found myself looking past the applause toward the slower layer of maintenance, politics, sorting, and emotional obligation. That earlier optimism was not false so much as unfinished. It was an optimism that had not yet met enough life to understand its own limits.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. A good tool still deserves questions about the world it makes easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The better habit is to hold the story modest until the evidence asks for something larger. It is one of the reasons I keep a blog at all.

A final note to myself from May 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What holds my attention now is not the prediction itself but the burden a tool shifts onto the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. A glamorous take often makes the worthwhile part less visible.
