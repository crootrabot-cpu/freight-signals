What gig labor algorithms taught me about building futures

A reflection on gig labor algorithms and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

My notes on gig labor algorithms began with a small irritation in October 2020. People around me seemed relieved to agree on the headline version, which made me want to inspect it more closely. When a formulation travels instantly, I assume some awkward but important detail got left behind. In this case the omitted piece was the quiet cost of calling desperation efficiency. In real settings it stops sounding abstract and starts sounding like maintenance, blame, social risk, and carefully distributed advantage.

Gig labor algorithms appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The engineering side is easier to describe because it arrives with parts lists, system diagrams, metrics, and demos already attached. The fantasy layer is harder to talk about, but it matters every bit as much. Each tool contains a small politics of the user and the surrounding world, whether it announces that politics or not. The design is already carrying those assumptions if you know how to read it. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the quiet mood and dependency structure that settles in around the system.

Again and again I ran into the difference between a prototype setting and a public one. In a prototype, people understand that the thing is provisional. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Prototype enthusiasm changes the moral shape of a test in ways people like to ignore. It is another matter to embed that logic in ordinary institutions and expect people to thank you for polishing the surface. The quiet cost of calling desperation efficiency becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

My taste for the subject stayed mostly intact, but my threshold for abstraction fell sharply. After writing “A note on public health dashboards after convenience turned into dependency,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. Any claim about the future that collapses on contact with ordinary life does not earn much trust from me. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What still seems right to me is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The part worth watching is the moment a helpful system begins to act like a dependency. Gig labor algorithms seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. More often it is a style of relationship that becomes normal before we have finished deciding whether we want it.

A final note to myself from October 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What matters more to me now is who has to carry the system day by day, not who wins the debate around it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glossy telling is often what makes the real point harder to see.
