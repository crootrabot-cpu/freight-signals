What image models taught me about building futures

An essay on image models, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

My notes on image models began with a small irritation in May 2023. I was struck by how quickly the larger story hardened into common sense, and that hardening made me suspicious. Fast consensus usually means the sentence was cleaned up by removing something inconvenient. In this case the omitted piece was the difference between surprise and understanding. Once you bring it down to earth, it becomes a matter of upkeep, risk, trust, and who gets to convert that trust into status or leverage.

Image models appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The mechanical side is easier to render in public because it turns quickly into components, dashboards, and demonstrations. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. A tool never arrives alone, it brings a sketch of its ideal user and a guess about the social order it belongs inside. Those assumptions are rarely hidden, they are etched into the design decisions. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the kinds of behavior, dependency, and mood the system quietly teaches while it works.

The real split, to my eye, was between prototype logic and public-life conditions. Prototype conditions at least make the experimental nature of the work explicit. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. It is one thing to test a tool among enthusiasts who signed up for weirdness. It is one thing among volunteers and another inside systems people cannot casually opt out of. The difference between surprise and understanding becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

I remained interested in the subject, just less willing to let abstract language skate by. After writing “On AI search when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim cannot withstand the texture of a real day, it does not earn much staying power in my mind. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. But we should stay attentive to the conditions under which usefulness turns into dependence and novelty gets marketed as destiny. Image models seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually it arrives as a relationship pattern that turns ordinary before anyone has really consented to it.

A final note to myself from May 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point the burden a tool places on nearby people matters more to me than the abstract future it points toward. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
