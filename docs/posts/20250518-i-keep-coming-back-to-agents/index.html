Agents make responsibility harder to point to

This essay treats agents less as magic workers and more as systems that blur accountability.

I wrote this down in May 2025 after thinking about agents from the vantage point of a morning spent comparing outputs instead of admiring them. What held me was not the headline version but the deeper question of what sort of world the idea thought it was entering. With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. Most discussions rush from capability to performance to scale and leave the question of livability trailing behind. Agents seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the feature everyone reached for in public explanations. When people talk in public they reach first for the visible layer, the thing that demos cleanly and fits in a headline. The social consequence tends to live in the surrounding effects more than in the banner feature. They change pacing. They reshape who gets to stay broad and who has to start translating themselves into machine-friendly terms. They change what falls through the cracks because the cracks themselves move. Once you notice that shift, agents stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

Atmosphere still matters more than product people like to admit. Some technologies give a person more room to think and move. Others turn every interaction into a low-grade compliance ritual. Benchmarks miss that difference all the time, but bodies do not. If a system keeps making life feel narrower and more procedural, I take that as part of its design rather than an accidental side effect.

Looking back from May 2025, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “On interface trust after the magic show,” from April 2025. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. The interesting part had shifted for me from the performance to the aftermath: upkeep, governance, sorting, and the psychological deal the system expected people to sign. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was the sort of incomplete optimism you get before the machinery has had time to reach enough people.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. I do not see any contradiction between admiring a tool and interrogating the world it quietly normalizes. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version usually starts by distrusting the oversized story until the smaller one has earned the right to expand. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On safety debates as the internet learned a new kind of performance.”

A final note to myself from May 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The argument about the future matters less now than the local demands the tool makes. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
