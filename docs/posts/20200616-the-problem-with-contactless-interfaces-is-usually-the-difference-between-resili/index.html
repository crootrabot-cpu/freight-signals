The problem with contactless interfaces is usually the difference between resilience and improvisation

A reflection on contactless interfaces and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

There was a version of contactless interfaces circulating in June 2020 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Often it is a story built from selective conditions rather than a stable reality. Standing inside an empty downtown street, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

One reason the topic stays interesting is that it will not settle into a clean ideological camp. Contactless interfaces can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. The mature version of this argument has to make room for both sides of that feeling. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. What matters to me is what the tool normalizes while everyone is busy applauding the convenience.

What struck me in 2020 was how social the whole thing had become. People were not just choosing tools, they were choosing atmospheres, affiliations, and self-descriptions through those tools. Some wanted relief from repetitive work. Some wanted legitimacy. Some wanted to feel early without feeling reckless. The evidence never moved as fast as those social needs did.

One thing experience has taught me is to notice what gets edited out when a story turns professional. Usually the missing pieces come from the same family: labor, error handling, emotional load, repair, governance, and unevenly distributed inconvenience. Contactless interfaces looked different to me once I framed it that way. Over time the sleekness gave way to the more familiar shape of trade-offs dressed for release. That does not make the thing useless. The first real task of criticism is sometimes just to make the thing understandable again.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It helps me watch the technologies change and my own standards of attention change with them. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What stays with me is the slower change in what feels ordinary, what feels parasitic, and what still deserves desire. Contactless interfaces sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from June 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I spend less time on the forecast now and more on the burden the tool lays on nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
