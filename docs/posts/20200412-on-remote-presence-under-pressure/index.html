On remote presence under pressure

A personal essay about remote presence, and the longer argument hidden inside software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit.

My notes on remote presence began with a small irritation in April 2020. People around me seemed relieved to agree on the headline version, which made me want to inspect it more closely. Quick agreement often means someone has already done the trimming required to make the thought portable. In this case the omitted piece was software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit. The phrase may sound high-level, but daily life translates it into maintenance work, absorbed errors, uneven trust, and lopsided reward.

Remote presence appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The engineering side is easier to describe because it arrives with parts lists, system diagrams, metrics, and demos already attached. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. Buried inside every tool is an expectation about the person it is for and the society it assumes. Those buried assumptions show up quite clearly in the choices the design makes. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the behavioral atmosphere the system smuggles in under the banner of functionality.

The important gap, to my eye, was between the prototype setting and the public setting beyond it. One virtue of prototype life is that everyone knows the terms are still experimental. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Testing among volunteers who actively wanted the weird version of the future is one thing. It is different once the same logic enters public life and people are asked to call it progress because the interface looks clean. Software stepping into spaces that used to belong to habit becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

I did not lose interest in the topic, I just lost patience for talking about it at altitude. After writing “The problem with fragile supply chains is usually the difference between resilience and improvisation,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim about the future cannot survive contact with somebody's day, their tools, their fatigue, and their incentives, I do not expect it to last. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. What matters is noticing when usefulness starts hardening into dependence and weirdness gets sold back to us as fate. Remote presence seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually what hardens is not the tool itself but a pattern of relationship around it.

A final note to myself from April 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I keep returning to the same question, who has to take this system home with them each day? If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
