On coordination systems when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite

A reflection on coordination systems and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in February 2021 after thinking about coordination systems from the vantage point of an empty downtown street. What held me was not the headline but the deeper question underneath it: what sort of world is this idea written for? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. We talk about capability before context, performance before maintenance, and scale before asking whether any of it makes life more livable. Coordination systems seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The mechanism that interested me most was not the one public explanations usually emphasized. Public explanations tend to favor the most visible element, the piece that fits in a headline or a screenshot. The part that becomes socially important is usually the second-order shift around the technology. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain complex and who has to tidy themselves up for the system. They change what can be ignored because a hidden workflow is now catching it. Once you notice that shift, coordination systems stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

Atmosphere still matters more than product people like to admit. Some technologies give a person more room to think and move. Others turn every interaction into a low-grade compliance ritual. Benchmarks miss that difference all the time, but bodies do not. If a system keeps making life feel narrower and more procedural, I take that as part of its design rather than an accidental side effect.

Looking back from February 2021, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “On telepresence rituals under pressure,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. I had become more interested in the system after the applause than during it, especially in the maintenance and social consequences that came next. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was a version of optimism that had not yet survived enough proximity.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can admire good tools without giving up the right to ask what kind of world they are quietly assembling. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by refusing the oversized story until the smaller one earns the right to expand. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “What communal tools taught me about building futures.”

A final note to myself from February 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The future matters to me less as prediction than as a map of what the system demands up close. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
