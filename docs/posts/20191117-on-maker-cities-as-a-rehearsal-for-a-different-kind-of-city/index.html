On maker cities as a rehearsal for a different kind of city

An essay on maker cities, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in November 2019 after thinking about maker cities from the vantage point of an overcaffeinated dinner where everyone had a sketchbook open. The surface topic mattered less than the background assumption about the world the idea expects to find. That is often the first thing dropped once the topic turns technical. We keep prioritizing capability, performance, and scale while deferring the question of whether the system improves daily life. Maker cities seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. The public version usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, robot, control surface. Most tools matter through the consequences they release, not the performance they stage. They change pacing. They decide who can go on operating through judgment and who must become parseable. They change what falls through the cracks because the cracks themselves move. Once you notice that shift, maker cities stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

I keep thinking about atmosphere because it reveals what numbers often cannot. One system leaves room for human judgment. Another turns the whole setting into a run of prompts, checks, and tiny permissions. You can feel that difference long before you can quantify it. What a tool makes includes the mood it leaves people inside.

Looking back from November 2019, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “What communal tools taught me about building futures,” from July 2019. At that point I was still describing what looked appealing on the surface. By then the interesting part was the system’s afterlife, not its stage presence. I would not say the earlier optimism was wrong, only early. The optimism was not false so much as early, formed before the machinery had touched enough people.

I do not think becoming anti-technology for self-protection solves the real problem. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Useful tools should invite scrutiny too, especially about the world they normalize. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version keeps the frame narrow until the world forces it wider. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On solar abundance as a rehearsal for a different kind of city.”

A final note to myself from November 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point being right about the future matters less than being honest about who carries the burden. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The prettier version is often the one that helps us miss the important thing.
