What benchmarks taught me about building futures

A post about benchmarks while taste and trust were both up for grabs, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

I wrote this down in October 2023 after thinking about benchmarks from the vantage point of a coffee shop conversation that turned into an accidental seminar. The surface topic mattered less than the background assumption about the world the idea expects to find. That tends to be the first thing technology talk smooths over. Most conversations rush from capability to performance to scale while the livability question trails behind. Benchmarks seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The socially important mechanism was not the one public explanations liked to point at first. The public version usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, robot, control surface. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They alter who may stay flexible and who must start performing legibility for the machine. They make different things visible or ignorable because the workflow beneath the surface has shifted. Once you notice that shift, benchmarks stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The question of atmosphere keeps drawing me back because metrics tend to leave it out. One system leaves room for human judgment. Another converts the setting into a corridor of prompts, confirmations, and minor approvals. That distinction may be tricky to count, but it is easy to sense. The emotional weather around a tool is one of its outputs, not just scenery.

Looking back from October 2023, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “The problem with AI search is usually the pressure to form a worldview too quickly,” from August 2023. That earlier note stayed closer to the surface appeal than this one does. The interesting part, for me, had moved past the applause and into maintenance, governance, social sorting, and the emotional deal the system expected people to accept. I cannot quite call that earlier optimism wrong. It was a version of optimism that had not yet survived enough proximity.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. There is no contradiction between appreciating a tool and interrogating the environment it builds. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The sane version keeps the frame narrow until the world forces it wider. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On telepresence rituals under pressure.”

A final note to myself from October 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I am more interested in the obligations a tool creates than in the prestige of a correct forecast. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The shiny version is often the easiest way to overlook what actually matters.
