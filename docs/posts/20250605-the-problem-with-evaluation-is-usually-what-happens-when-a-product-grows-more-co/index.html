Most evaluation problems are really trust problems

An essay on evaluation as a trust problem, not just a metrics problem.

My notes on evaluation began with a small irritation in June 2025. What unsettled me was not disagreement but how easily everyone seemed to agree on the headline version of events. When everyone repeats the same clean formulation, I assume some rough but important detail has been left on the cutting-room floor. In this case the omitted piece was what happens when a product grows more competent than legible. In practice the issue surfaces through plain details, like who keeps the system running, who bears the cost of failure, and who has the least choice about trusting it.

Evaluation appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. Talking about the engineering is straightforward because it already knows how to present itself. The part that lives in imagination is harder to name, but it matters just as much. Buried in every tool is an assumption about who will use it well and what kind of social environment should feel natural around it. Those assumptions sit in the design for anyone willing to notice them. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also a question of what kinds of dependency and behavior the system makes feel ordinary.

I kept noticing the moral distance between a prototype and the public environment it eventually enters. Prototype settings come with a crucial honesty: everyone knows it is still an experiment. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Trying something first among people who actively wanted the strange version is one thing. It is another step entirely to turn that logic into public infrastructure and hope presentation smooths over the burden. What happens when a product grows more competent than legible becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

I did not lose interest in the topic, I lost patience for talking about it at altitude. After writing “On interface trust after the magic show,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a future-facing claim cannot survive contact with somebody's actual day, their tools, their fatigue, and their incentives, I stop trusting it pretty quickly. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What matters to me is proportion, not the fantasy of purity. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The part worth watching is the slide from convenience into obligation, especially when the slide is narrated as progress. Evaluation seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Most of the time the shift is relational before it is technological, it becomes normal before it becomes discussed.

A final note to myself from June 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I no longer find the forecast itself as interesting as the human bargain each tool quietly proposes to its nearest users. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous telling often comes at the cost of noticing the important part.
