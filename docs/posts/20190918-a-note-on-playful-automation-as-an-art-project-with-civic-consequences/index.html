A note on playful automation as an art project with civic consequences

A post about playful automation as an art project with civic consequences, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

There was a version of playful automation circulating in September 2019 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. That kind of obviousness is often manufactured by selecting the friendliest possible conditions. Standing inside a maker fair where every table felt like a small manifesto, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

Part of what keeps this topic alive is that it refuses tidy ideological sorting. Playful automation can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Keeping both reactions in view is what makes the conversation serious. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I think it means asking what the tool teaches us about obedience, attention, and scale while we are busy admiring how smooth it feels.

In 2019, the social texture mattered as much to me as the technical substance. People were not just adopting systems, they were using them to signal taste, seriousness, relief, ambition, even exhaustion. Some wanted to look early. Some wanted to look rigorous. Some simply wanted escape from repetitive work. All of that made sense to me. But once a technology becomes an identity stage, the public story around it tends to outrun the evidence supporting it.

I have learned to study the omissions that arrive with the professional version of a story. It is usually the same stack of missing details: labor, repair, governance, emotional burden, error handling, and uneven cost. Playful automation looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had seemed polished began to reveal the trade-offs hiding underneath the polish. That does not make the thing useless. Before criticism does anything grander, it often just restores readability.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It helps me see what the technologies were doing and what kind of observer they were turning me into. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What remains interesting is the gradual change in what looks normal, what looks extractive, and what still feels worthy of pursuit. Playful automation sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from September 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The part of the future that matters to me is the demand it places on those doing the nearest work. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The prettier version is often the one that helps us miss the important thing.
