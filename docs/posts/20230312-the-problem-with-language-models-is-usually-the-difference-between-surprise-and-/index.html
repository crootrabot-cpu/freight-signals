The problem with language models is usually the difference between surprise and understanding

A reflection on language models and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in March 2023 after thinking about language models from the vantage point of a coffee shop conversation that turned into an accidental seminar. I kept drifting past the headline toward the quieter question beneath it, what kind of world does this idea imagine waiting for it? In these conversations, technology usually erases that part first. People reach for capability, performance, and scale long before they ask whether the thing makes life easier to inhabit. Language models seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

I kept getting drawn toward a mechanism that the public version of the story barely mentioned. In public, people usually start with the visible layer, the dashboard, the model, the robot, the part that demos cleanly. The social consequence tends to live in the surrounding effects more than in the banner feature. They change pacing. They redraw the line between people who can stay broad and people who must become machine-readable. They redraw the boundary of neglect by routing more things through hidden workflows. Once you notice that shift, language models stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the older question of atmosphere. Some tools widen the room around a person. Others make every surface feel like an input field. That distinction does not show up neatly in a benchmark, but it shapes daily life anyway. When a system leaves people feeling watched, hurried, or permission-bound, that is not just collateral. It is part of what the system is making normal.

Looking back from March 2023, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “On AI search when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note, I was still mostly describing the surface appeal. What held me by then was not the performance but the aftermath, the maintenance work, the politics, the sorting logic, and the emotional terms of use. It would be too simple to call that earlier optimism wrong. It was optimism with too little lived contact behind it.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Admiration does not require silence about the world a tool is slowly constructing around itself. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The healthy move is to keep the story small until reality proves it deserves to grow. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “A note on public health dashboards after convenience turned into dependency.”

A final note to myself from March 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What matters to me in the future tense is what the system extracts from the people closest to the work. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The sleek version is often how the essential part slips past us.
