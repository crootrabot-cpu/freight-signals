A note on human-scale robotics when everything still felt handmade

An essay on human-scale robotics, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in November 2018 after thinking about human-scale robotics from the vantage point of a quiet walk through Oakland after midnight. The headline claim interested me less than the older question below it: what sort of world is this idea already presuming? That tends to disappear first once technology enters the sentence. The usual sequence favors capability over context, performance over upkeep, and scale over livability. Human-scale robotics seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The socially important mechanism was not the one public explanations liked to point at first. Public talk tends to reach first for the visible move, the interface, the model, the robot, the service layer. Most tools matter through the consequences they release, not the performance they stage. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a person with context and who must become a neat input. They redraw the boundary of neglect by routing more things through hidden workflows. Once you notice that shift, human-scale robotics stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

Atmosphere still matters more than product people like to admit. Some technologies give a person more room to think and move. Others turn every interaction into a low-grade compliance ritual. Benchmarks miss that difference all the time, but bodies do not. If a system keeps making life feel narrower and more procedural, I take that as part of its design rather than an accidental side effect.

Looking back from November 2018, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “In Dust We Trust,” from September 2018. That earlier note was still occupied with the thing’s surface appeal. I was paying closer attention here to the post-demo layer: upkeep, politics, sorting logic, and the emotional contract hidden inside the workflow. I do not read that earlier optimism as a mistake, exactly. It was an optimism that had not yet met enough life to understand its own limits.

This is not me making the case for defensive anti-technology. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. A good tool still deserves questions about the world it makes easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by refusing the oversized story until the smaller one earns the right to expand. It is one of the reasons I keep a blog at all.

A final note to myself from November 2018: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I am less occupied by who wins the discussion than by who ends up living inside the system. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. A glamorous telling usually conceals the thing worth looking at.
