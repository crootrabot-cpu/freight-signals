The internet after AI still feels unfinished to me

A closing essay on the internet after AI, written from the feeling that the medium is still deciding what it wants to become.

There was a version of the internet after AI circulating in April 2026 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. The trend sounds obvious only because somebody has already done the quiet work of narrowing the scene. Standing inside a Sunday morning with fewer tabs open than usual, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep finding the topic interesting because it refuses to line up cleanly with anyone’s preferred camp. The internet after AI can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A grown-up conversation has to hold both truths at once. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I take the question to be about what kinds of obedience and attention the tool normalizes under the cover of convenience.

By 2026, the systems were doing double duty as tools and as social signals. People were reaching for them because they solved problems, but also because they implied something flattering about the user. That is where my caution kicked in. Once people start borrowing identity from a system, the claims around it tend to grow faster than the proof.

One thing I watch for now is what disappears once a story starts getting repeated in polished settings. Usually the missing pieces are familiar: labor, repair, emotional load, error handling, governance, and who ends up carrying the inconvenience. The internet after AI looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had looked sleek at first started to read more like a stack of trade-offs wearing good marketing. That does not make the thing useless. Sometimes making a system legible again is the first real job criticism does well.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It shows me the technologies, yes, but also the evolution of what I considered worth noticing. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What lasts is the slower drift in what feels ordinary, what feels parasitic, and what still feels worth wanting. The internet after AI sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from April 2026: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The part I keep returning to is not the prediction but the burden placed on the people expected to absorb the tool into daily life. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
