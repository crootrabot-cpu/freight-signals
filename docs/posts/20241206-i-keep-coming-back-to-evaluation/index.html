I keep coming back to evaluation

A reflection on evaluation and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in December 2024 after thinking about evaluation from the vantage point of an office where the exciting part was no longer the model but the glue. I was less interested in the headline version of the topic than in the tougher question underneath it: what kind of world does this idea think it is entering? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. Most conversations rush from capability to performance to scale while the livability question trails behind. Evaluation seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

I kept getting drawn toward a mechanism that the public version of the story barely mentioned. Public language likes the visible layer, the robot, the interface, the model, the thing that screenshots well. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They decide who can go on operating through judgment and who must become parseable. They redraw the boundary of neglect by routing more things through hidden workflows. Once you notice that shift, evaluation stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

The atmosphere around a tool is not decorative, it is part of the thing being built. Some systems create room for judgment and improvisation. Others turn ordinary life into a corridor of tiny permissions. That shift can be difficult to benchmark, but it is impossible to live inside without noticing. The mood around a system tells you something real about its politics.

Looking back from December 2024, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “A note on local inference when reliability turned out to be the whole game,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. I was paying closer attention here to the post-demo layer: upkeep, politics, sorting logic, and the emotional contract hidden inside the workflow. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was optimism with too little lived contact behind it.

The goal, to me, is not anti-technology purity. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Useful tools should invite scrutiny too, especially about the world they normalize. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The healthy move is to keep the story small until reality proves it deserves to grow. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “On public health dashboards when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite.”

A final note to myself from December 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I do not find the argument about what comes next as gripping as the pressure a tool puts on the people living beside it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
