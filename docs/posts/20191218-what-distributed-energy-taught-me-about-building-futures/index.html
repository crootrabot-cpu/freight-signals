What distributed energy taught me about building futures

A personal essay about distributed energy, and the longer argument hidden inside how quickly spectacle outruns stewardship.

There was a version of distributed energy circulating in December 2019 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. It is usually a story told after somebody has quietly selected the conditions that make the trend look obvious. Standing inside a quiet walk through Oakland after midnight, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I return to it because it misbehaves inside the usual ideological categories. Distributed energy can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Keeping both reactions in view is what makes the conversation serious. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I read the question as one about what kinds of compliance and attention the tool normalizes under the cover of ease.

By 2019, it was obvious that the social layer mattered as much as the technical one. These systems were not only tools, they were ways of performing judgment, modernity, competence, and sometimes hope. People wanted relief, status, clarity, momentum, or just a way out of dull work. That is understandable. The danger is that identity and evidence start moving at different speeds, and institutions often side with identity first.

The longer I do this, the more I watch the details that fall away once the polished retelling begins. Most of the absent material lives in the same neighborhood: labor, maintenance, emotional cost, repair, governance, and asymmetric inconvenience. Distributed energy looked different to me once I framed it that way. What had first appeared smooth began to show its seams. That does not make the thing useless. Sometimes criticism is valuable simply because it makes the object legible again.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It lets me read the archive as a record of the technologies and of my own changing eye. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. The part that lasts is the slower movement in what becomes normal, what becomes extractive, and what continues to feel worth wanting. Distributed energy sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from December 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I read the future mainly through the demands a tool places on its closest users. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous telling often comes at the cost of noticing the important part.
