A note on prompt rituals while the hype still outran the evidence

A personal essay about prompt rituals, and the longer argument hidden inside how language softens around systems we do not yet know how to test.

My notes on prompt rituals began with a small irritation in April 2023. Everyone around me seemed strangely satisfied with the headline version, which only made me more suspicious. When a formulation travels instantly, I assume some awkward but important detail got left behind. In this case the omitted piece was how language softens around systems we do not yet know how to test. That phrasing may sound abstract, but it cashes out in very ordinary questions about maintenance, blame, trust, and who gets rewarded for calling all of that progress.

Prompt rituals appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The mechanical side is easier to render in public because it turns quickly into components, dashboards, and demonstrations. The imaginative part is trickier to name, but it matters just as much. A tool never arrives alone, it brings a sketch of its ideal user and a guess about the social order it belongs inside. Look carefully and the design shows those assumptions plainly enough. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the emotional and behavioral terms on which the system asks to be accepted.

I kept noticing the moral distance between a prototype and the public environment it eventually enters. In a prototype, people understand that the thing is provisional. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. There is a big difference between testing on willing enthusiasts and asking ordinary people to inherit the result. It is another scale of responsibility entirely once the logic is folded into places people depend on. How language softens around systems we do not yet know how to test becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

My taste for the subject stayed mostly intact, but my threshold for abstraction fell sharply. After writing “On AI search when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. I trust claims about the future only when they can survive the messier terms of actual life. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What still seems right to me is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The key thing is to watch for the point where a useful thing stops feeling optional. Prompt rituals seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. It is usually the relational pattern that hardens, not the tool in isolation.

A final note to myself from April 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I care less about being right about the future than about what a tool quietly asks of the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The theatrical version tends to obscure the part that really matters.
