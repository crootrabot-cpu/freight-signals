The problem with workflow automation is usually the difference between an assistant and a dependency

A personal essay about workflow automation, and the longer argument hidden inside the difference between an assistant and a dependency.

My notes on workflow automation began with a small irritation in March 2024. The more universally the big story was repeated, the less comfortable I felt inside it. If everyone repeats the same polished line, I assume some important grit has already been swept away. In this case the omitted piece was the difference between an assistant and a dependency. From a distance it sounds conceptual, but nearby it turns into a question about who keeps the system afloat and who pays for its mistakes.

Workflow automation appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The engineering story is easy to tell because it arrives with visible pieces and measurable claims. The imaginative side is slipperier to explain, but it matters every bit as much. The tool arrives with an image of its user already drawn in, along with a preferred backdrop for that user. Those assumptions are rarely hidden, they are etched into the design decisions. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the style of dependence the system teaches while everyone is busy calling it useful.

I kept noticing the moral distance between a prototype and the public environment it eventually enters. Inside a prototype, everyone knows they are participating in a test. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. The first audience is often self-selected for novelty, which makes the test environment gentler than public life. It is much easier to defend the logic in a demo than in the places where people actually have to live with it. The difference between an assistant and a dependency becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

I remained interested in the subject, just less willing to let abstract language skate by. After writing “I keep coming back to local inference,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim about the future cannot survive contact with somebody's day, their tools, their fatigue, and their incentives, I do not expect it to last. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I am after is not purity so much as proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. I mostly want us to notice the slide from convenience into obligation. Workflow automation seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. More often the shift is relational before it is technological, and it becomes normal before it becomes examined.

A final note to myself from March 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What interests me now is less the prediction than the weight a tool places on its immediate users. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The shiny version is often the easiest way to overlook what actually matters.
