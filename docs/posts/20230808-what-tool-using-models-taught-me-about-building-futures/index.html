What tool-using models taught me about building futures

A personal essay about tool-using models, and the longer argument hidden inside the difference between surprise and understanding.

There was a version of tool-using models circulating in August 2023 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Usually that kind of inevitability appears only after someone has chosen a very flattering frame. Standing inside a coffee shop conversation that turned into an accidental seminar, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep circling back to it because it refuses clean ideological categories. Tool-using models can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A serious version of the conversation has to keep both reactions in view. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. To me it means asking what habits of obedience and attention the tool installs while we celebrate the convenience.

By 2023, the systems were doing double duty as tools and as social signals. People reached for them partly because they solved problems and partly because they said something flattering about the person using them. That is where my caution kicked in. Once people use a system as self-description, the claims around it usually outrun what has been shown.

The longer I do this, the more I watch the details that fall away once the polished retelling begins. The omissions are usually familiar, labor, maintenance, repair, emotional burden, governance, failure handling, and who absorbs the inconvenience. Tool-using models looked different to me once I framed it that way. What looked clean at first eventually read like trade-offs with a competent publicist. That does not make the thing useless. Sometimes the most practical gift criticism provides is renewed legibility.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It lets me track not only the movement of the technologies but the movement of my own noticing. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What remains interesting is the gradual change in what looks normal, what looks extractive, and what still feels worthy of pursuit. Tool-using models sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from August 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The forecast has become less interesting to me than the burden a tool lays on ordinary users. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The polished version is often exactly how the crucial part gets missed.
