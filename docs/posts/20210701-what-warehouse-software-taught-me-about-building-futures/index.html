What warehouse software taught me about building futures

A personal essay about warehouse software, and the longer argument hidden inside how coordination can feel intimate and brutal at the same time.

I wrote this down in June 2021 after thinking about warehouse software from the vantage point of a late-night browser tab spiral full of charts and shipping estimates. The better question, for me, was not the headline one but the one underneath it: what kind of life is this idea assuming? That is often the first thing dropped once the topic turns technical. People reach for capability, performance, and scale long before they ask whether the thing makes life easier to inhabit. Warehouse software seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

What interested me most was not the obvious mechanism from the demo version. Public language likes the visible layer, the robot, the interface, the model, the thing that screenshots well. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They alter who may stay flexible and who must start performing legibility for the machine. They make different things visible or ignorable because the workflow beneath the surface has shifted. Once you notice that shift, warehouse software stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the older question of atmosphere. Some tools widen the room around a person. Others make every surface feel like an input field. That distinction does not show up neatly in a benchmark, but it shapes daily life anyway. When a system leaves people feeling watched, hurried, or permission-bound, that is not just collateral. It is part of what the system is making normal.

Looking back from June 2021, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “On mutual aid apps when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. The earlier version of me was still describing the immediate appeal. What drew me now was everything that arrived after the applause died down. I do not think the earlier optimism was mistaken so much as incomplete. Nothing about it was dishonest, it was simply the kind of optimism that arrives before enough contact.

I am not trying to argue anyone into an anti-technology stance out of self-defense. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. A good tool still deserves questions about the world it makes easier to inhabit. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The healthy move is to keep the story small until reality proves it deserves to grow. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “A note on playful automation as an art project with civic consequences.”

A final note to myself from June 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point the burden a tool places on nearby people matters more to me than the abstract future it points toward. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The flashy framing often lures attention away from the important part.
