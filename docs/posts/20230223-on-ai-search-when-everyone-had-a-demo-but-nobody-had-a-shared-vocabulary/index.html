On AI search when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary

A post about AI search when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

My notes on AI search began with a small irritation in February 2023. Everyone around me seemed strangely satisfied with the headline version, which only made me more suspicious. Consensus has a habit of sounding elegant only after the messier facts have been tucked away. In this case the omitted piece was what gets lost when the demo becomes the argument. That phrasing may sound abstract, but it cashes out in very ordinary questions about maintenance, blame, trust, and who gets rewarded for calling all of that progress.

AI search appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. It is easier to talk about the engineering because engineering already knows how to present itself. The fantasy side is less convenient to discuss, though it matters no less. Tools show up carrying assumptions about who will use them well and what kind of world should make that use feel natural. Those buried assumptions show up quite clearly in the choices the design makes. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about what forms of behavior and reliance the system makes seem natural.

Again and again I ran into the difference between a prototype setting and a public one. The prototype has one advantage, nobody mistakes it for settled reality. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. A test run among willing enthusiasts is one thing. It is much easier to defend the logic in a demo than in the places where people actually have to live with it. What gets lost when the demo becomes the argument becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

What changed most was not my interest in the topic but my tolerance for abstraction. After writing “I keep coming back to tool-using models,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. Once a future-facing claim fails contact with ordinary life, I assume the problem is with the claim rather than the day. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

I am not after purity. I am after proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The part worth watching is the moment a helpful system begins to act like a dependency. AI search seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. What usually hardens is less the tool than the relationship pattern it creates.

A final note to myself from February 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The argument about what comes next grips me less than the pressure a tool places on the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The staged version often pushes the real point just out of frame.
