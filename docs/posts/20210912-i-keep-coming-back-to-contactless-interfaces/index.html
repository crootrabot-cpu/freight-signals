I keep coming back to contactless interfaces

An essay on contactless interfaces, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in September 2021 after thinking about contactless interfaces from the vantage point of a video call where everyone looked slightly ghosted by their own webcam. I kept drifting past the headline toward the quieter question beneath it, what kind of world does this idea imagine waiting for it? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. The usual sequence favors capability over context, performance over upkeep, and scale over livability. Contactless interfaces seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The mechanism I cared about sat slightly to the side of the official explanation. Public explanations tend to favor the most visible element, the piece that fits in a headline or a screenshot. The broader consequence usually comes from what the technology sets loose around itself. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a generalist and who has to become legible to the machine. They make different things visible or ignorable because the workflow beneath the surface has shifted. Once you notice that shift, contactless interfaces stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the older question of atmosphere. Some tools widen the room around a person. Others make every surface feel like an input field. That distinction does not show up neatly in a benchmark, but it shapes daily life anyway. When a system leaves people feeling watched, hurried, or permission-bound, that is not just collateral. It is part of what the system is making normal.

Looking back from September 2021, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “On mutual aid apps when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite,” from June 2021. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. What interested me here was the aftermath of the applause: the maintenance burden, the politics, the quiet sorting, and the emotional contract being drafted between people and the system. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was a version of optimism that had not yet survived enough proximity.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can admire good tools without giving up the right to ask what kind of world they are quietly assembling. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It begins by distrusting the inflated version until the smaller account earns more room. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “I keep coming back to desert prototypes.”

A final note to myself from September 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I am more interested in the obligations a tool creates than in the prestige of a correct forecast. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
