Personal AI gets worse the moment it becomes generic

A post about personal AI and the way generic product logic can ruin something meant to feel specific.

I wrote this down in December 2025 after thinking about personal AI from the vantage point of a desktop crowded with small useful automations. The surface topic was not the real hook for me. The better question was what kind of life the idea seemed to assume in advance. With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. We default to capability talk, then performance talk, then scale talk, while the livability question gets deferred again. Personal AI seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

I kept getting pulled toward a different mechanism than the one the public conversation liked to emphasize. When people talk in public they reach first for the visible layer, the thing that demos cleanly and fits in a headline. The real social force often sits one step away from the capability people market first. They change pacing. They change the social split between people who get to stay complex and people who must become easy for the machine to parse. They make different things visible or ignorable because the workflow beneath the surface has shifted. Once you notice that shift, personal AI stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is a version of this argument that ignores atmosphere, and I do not trust it. Some systems open up breathing room. Others make every action feel slightly supervised. Measurement rarely captures the difference cleanly, but daily life does. When a tool thickens procedure and shrinks the room people feel around themselves, that belongs in the evaluation.

Looking back from December 2025, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “I keep coming back to interface trust,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. I was paying more attention now to the afterlife of the system, the maintenance, the politics, the sorting logic, and the emotional terms of participation. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. It was incomplete in the familiar way optimism becomes incomplete before the machinery has touched enough real lives.

I do not think the answer is to become anti-technology as a form of self-protection. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can like a tool and still ask what sort of world it is slowly arranging around itself. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The better habit is to keep the frame modest until the evidence actually asks for something bigger. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “A note on chat interfaces before the story settled.”

A final note to myself from December 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The part I keep returning to is not the prediction but the burden placed on the people expected to absorb the tool into daily life. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
