On coordination systems under pressure

A post about coordination systems under pressure, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

There was a version of coordination systems circulating in September 2020 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. Usually that kind of inevitability appears only after someone has chosen a very flattering frame. Standing inside a kitchen table covered in charging cables, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I return to it because it misbehaves inside the usual ideological categories. Coordination systems can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. Any adult conversation about it has to hold those two responses together. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. I think it means asking what the tool teaches us about obedience, attention, and scale while we are busy admiring how smooth it feels.

What struck me in 2020 was how social the whole thing had become. They were choosing tools as well as the atmospheres and self-descriptions that came with them. Some wanted relief from repetitive work. Some wanted legitimacy. Some wanted to feel early without feeling reckless. Evidence could not keep up with the speed of those social needs.

I have learned to study the omissions that arrive with the professional version of a story. It is often the same cluster of absences: labor, upkeep, emotional load, repair, governance, and lopsided inconvenience. Coordination systems looked different to me once I framed it that way. The sleek solution slowly resolved into a stack of trade-offs wearing persuasive language. That does not make the thing useless. Legibility is often the first service criticism performs well.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It shows me both the technologies and the slow education of my own attention. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What stays interesting to me is the slower drift in what starts to feel normal, what starts to feel extractive, and what still seems worth wanting. Coordination systems sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from September 2020: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I care less now about forecasting correctly than about understanding the burden a tool places on the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous story usually distracts from the part that counts.
