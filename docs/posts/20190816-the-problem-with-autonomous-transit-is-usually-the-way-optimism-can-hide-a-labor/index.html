The problem with autonomous transit is usually the way optimism can hide a labor model

A personal essay about autonomous transit, and the longer argument hidden inside the way optimism can hide a labor model.

I wrote this down in August 2019 after thinking about autonomous transit from the vantage point of the desert at sunrise. What held me was not the headline but the deeper question underneath it: what sort of world is this idea written for? Technology conversations usually lose that part first. The usual sequence favors capability over context, performance over upkeep, and scale over livability. Autonomous transit seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The mechanism I cared about sat slightly to the side of the official explanation. In public, people usually start with the visible layer, the dashboard, the model, the robot, the part that demos cleanly. Most technologies become socially important through their second-order effects, not their slogans. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a person with context and who must become a neat input. What goes ignored changes when hidden workflows start doing the catching. Once you notice that shift, autonomous transit stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

Atmosphere still matters more than product people like to admit. Some technologies give a person more room to think and move. Others turn every interaction into a low-grade compliance ritual. Benchmarks miss that difference all the time, but bodies do not. If a system keeps making life feel narrower and more procedural, I take that as part of its design rather than an accidental side effect.

Looking back from August 2019, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “What communal tools taught me about building futures,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note, I was still mostly describing the surface appeal. I had become more interested in the system after the applause than during it, especially in the maintenance and social consequences that came next. That earlier optimism does not strike me as wrong, exactly. The problem was not optimism itself but how little reality it had yet encountered.

This is not an argument for anti-technology reflexes. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can admire good tools without giving up the right to ask what kind of world they are quietly assembling. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The healthy move is to keep the story small until reality proves it deserves to grow. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “I keep coming back to repair culture.”

A final note to myself from August 2019: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. The argument itself has become less interesting to me than the people who must incorporate the system into life. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous take often buries the part worth noticing.
