What gets delegated when you let agents act for you

A piece about agents, delegation, and the uncomfortable politics hiding inside automation that acts on your behalf.

I wrote this down in January 2025 after thinking about agents from the vantage point of an office where the exciting part was no longer the model but the glue. The topic itself was less interesting than the background assumption it carried about the world it expected to find. With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. People reach first for capability, then performance, then scale, and only much later for the question of whether the thing improves daily life. Agents seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The public version kept pointing at one mechanism, but it was another one that felt socially decisive to me. When people talk in public they reach first for the visible layer, the thing that demos cleanly and fits in a headline. But most technologies become socially important through a second-order effect. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a person with context and who has to become a tidy input. They change what can be ignored because some hidden workflow now catches it. Once you notice that shift, agents stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

There is also the old problem of atmosphere. Some technologies make people feel more room around themselves. Others make every surface feel like an input. Benchmarks are not built to capture that difference very well, so it is easy for people to miss. But mood is one of the outputs. A system that steadily constricts attention or multiplies little permissions is telling you something honest about itself. It is part of what the product is. The moment someone treats human texture as optional, I stop trusting the frame they are using.

Looking back from January 2025, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “A note on local inference when reliability turned out to be the whole game,” from November 2024. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. This time I cared less about the applause and more about what followed it: maintenance, politics, social sorting, and the emotional contract between people and the systems arranging their choices. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. Nothing about it was dishonest, it was simply the kind of optimism that arrives before enough lived contact.

I do not think the goal is to become anti-technology out of self-defense. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. We can like a tool and still ask what sort of world it is slowly arranging around itself. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. I trust writing more when it keeps the story small until the world insists otherwise. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “What telepresence rituals taught me about building futures.”

A final note to myself from January 2025: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I no longer find the forecast itself as interesting as the human bargain each tool quietly proposes to its nearest users. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
