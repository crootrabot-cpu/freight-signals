On tool-using models as the internet learned a new kind of performance

A personal essay about tool-using models, and the longer argument hidden inside how novelty can impersonate depth.

There was a version of tool-using models circulating in February 2023 that sounded inevitable. I have become suspicious of inevitability as a tone. That kind of obviousness is often manufactured by selecting the friendliest possible conditions. Standing inside a browser window full of generated images, what I noticed instead was contingency: how much of the future depends on atmosphere, labor, trust, and timing, and how often those human variables are treated as background noise once a technical idea starts sounding important.

I keep circling back to it because it refuses clean ideological categories. Tool-using models can look emancipatory when you focus on drudgery leaving the picture, and unsettling when you look at how quickly convenience hardens into expectation. Both reactions are real. A real conversation has to tolerate both reactions without flattening either one. I do not think skepticism means refusing tools. To me it means asking what habits of obedience and attention the tool installs while we celebrate the convenience.

By 2023, it was obvious that the social layer mattered as much as the technical one. These systems were not only tools, they were ways of performing judgment, modernity, competence, and sometimes hope. People wanted relief, status, clarity, momentum, or just a way out of dull work. That is understandable. The danger is that identity and evidence start moving at different speeds, and institutions often side with identity first.

The longer I do this, the more I watch the details that fall away once the polished retelling begins. The gaps are often made of the same materials: labor, upkeep, failure handling, emotional load, repair, and unequal inconvenience. Tool-using models looked different to me once I framed it that way. The sleek solution slowly resolved into a stack of trade-offs wearing persuasive language. That does not make the thing useless. Legibility is often the first service criticism performs well.

Maybe that is the real use of an archive like this one. It reveals both the tools and the evolution of what I considered worth noticing. The tools change. The language changes. The market changes its costume every six months. What endures for me is the slower rearrangement of what feels ordinary, parasitic, and still worth wanting. Tool-using models sat right in the middle of that drift, which is why I kept turning it over until it revealed more than its own sales pitch.

A final note to myself from February 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What stays in focus for me now is not the headline forecast but the burden a tool pushes onto ordinary people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The louder version usually pulls attention away from what matters.
