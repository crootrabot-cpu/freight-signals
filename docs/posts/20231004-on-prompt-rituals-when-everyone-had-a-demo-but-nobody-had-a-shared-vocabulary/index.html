On prompt rituals when everyone had a demo but nobody had a shared vocabulary

A personal essay about prompt rituals, and the longer argument hidden inside what gets lost when the demo becomes the argument.

My notes on prompt rituals began with a small irritation in October 2023. The easy agreement around the big story felt like a warning, not a comfort. Fast consensus usually means the sentence was cleaned up by removing something inconvenient. In this case the omitted piece was what gets lost when the demo becomes the argument. On the ground, the abstraction dissolves into plain details like upkeep, error burden, sequencing of trust, and who gets to narrate the whole thing as improvement.

Prompt rituals appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. The engineering side is easier to describe because it arrives with parts lists, system diagrams, metrics, and demos already attached. The more speculative part is harder to narrate, but it matters just as much. Tools carry assumptions not only about function but about the people meant to use them and the world meant to contain them. The design choices usually tell on those assumptions if you read them closely. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the kinds of submission, rhythm, and expectation the system normalizes.

I kept coming back to the distance between prototype life and public life. Prototype conditions at least make the experimental nature of the work explicit. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Testing among volunteers who actively wanted the weird version of the future is one thing. It is another thing to build the same logic into institutions and hope design language can hide the cost. What gets lost when the demo becomes the argument becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

What changed most was not my interest in the topic but my tolerance for abstraction. After writing “The problem with AI search is usually the pressure to form a worldview too quickly,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If the idea cannot make it through a real day, with its tools, fatigue, and incentives, I stop trusting it pretty quickly. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

The goal, for me, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The part worth watching is the moment a helpful system begins to act like a dependency. Prompt rituals seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. What becomes durable is often less the tool than the pattern of relationship it makes routine.

A final note to myself from October 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. These days I care less about winning the forecast and more about reading the burden the tool places on nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The sleek version is often how the essential part slips past us.
