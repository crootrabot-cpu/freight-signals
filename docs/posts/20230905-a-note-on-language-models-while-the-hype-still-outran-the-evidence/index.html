A note on language models while the hype still outran the evidence

A reflection on language models and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

I wrote this down in September 2023 after thinking about language models from the vantage point of a quiet hour reading papers with too many grand nouns. What held me was not the headline but the deeper question underneath it: what sort of world is this idea written for? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. The order is almost always capability first, then performance, then scale, and only later the question of livability. Language models seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The part that mattered to me was not the mechanism people tended to highlight in public. In public, the visible part gets all the attention, the robot, the interface, the model, the shiny layer on top. The part that becomes socially important is usually the second-order shift around the technology. They change pacing. They decide who can go on operating through judgment and who must become parseable. They alter what slips by unnoticed because some workflow in the background now captures it. Once you notice that shift, language models stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

I do not think atmosphere is a soft concern. It is where many technologies tell the truth about themselves. Some leave people feeling more capable and less managed. Others quietly convert daily life into a set of nudges, checks, and approvals. That effect may not show up in a benchmark chart, but it absolutely shows up in the lived experience of the system.

Looking back from September 2023, I could already feel my own position shifting. A few months earlier I had written “The problem with AI search is usually the pressure to form a worldview too quickly,” and the difference between that piece and this one is instructive. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. By then the interesting part was the system’s afterlife, not its stage presence. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. Nothing about it was dishonest, it was simply the kind of optimism that arrives before enough contact.

None of this adds up to a case for becoming anti-technology. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. It should be possible to like a tool and still question the world it is making normal. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The better habit is to hold the story modest until the evidence asks for something larger. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “The problem with delivery logistics is usually the difference between resilience and improvisation.”

A final note to myself from September 2023: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I keep returning to the same question, who has to take this system home with them each day? If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
