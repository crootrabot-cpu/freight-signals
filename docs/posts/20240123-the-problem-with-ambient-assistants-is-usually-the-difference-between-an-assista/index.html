The problem with ambient assistants is usually the difference between an assistant and a dependency

An essay on ambient assistants, with one eye on the machinery and one eye on the people asked to live with it.

I wrote this down in January 2024 after thinking about ambient assistants from the vantage point of a desktop crowded with small useful automations. The headline claim interested me less than the older question below it: what sort of world is this idea already presuming? In these conversations, technology usually erases that part first. The usual sequence favors capability over context, performance over upkeep, and scale over livability. Ambient assistants seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The socially important mechanism was not the one public explanations liked to point at first. In public, people usually start with the visible layer, the dashboard, the model, the robot, the part that demos cleanly. The broader consequence usually comes from what the technology sets loose around itself. They change pacing. They change who gets to remain a generalist and who has to become legible to the machine. They redraw the boundary of neglect by routing more things through hidden workflows. Once you notice that shift, ambient assistants stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

Atmosphere still matters more than product people like to admit. Some technologies give a person more room to think and move. Others turn every interaction into a low-grade compliance ritual. Benchmarks miss that difference all the time, but bodies do not. If a system keeps making life feel narrower and more procedural, I take that as part of its design rather than an accidental side effect.

Looking back from January 2024, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “A note on image models while the hype still outran the evidence,” from November 2023. Back in that earlier piece, I was still focused on the surface attraction. What held me by then was not the performance but the aftermath, the maintenance work, the politics, the sorting logic, and the emotional terms of use. The earlier optimism was not exactly wrong to my mind. The optimism needed more contact with actual use before it could become wisdom.

This is not an argument for an anti-technology stance born of self-protection. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. It should be possible to like a tool and still question the world it is making normal. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. The better habit is to hold the story modest until the evidence asks for something larger. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “The problem with gig labor algorithms is usually how coordination can feel intimate and brutal at the same time.”

A final note to myself from January 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What holds my attention now is not the prediction itself but the burden a tool shifts onto the people nearest it. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glossy telling is often what makes the real point harder to see.
