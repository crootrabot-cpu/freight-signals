A note on remote education tools when the infrastructure finally became visible

A post about remote education tools when the infrastructure finally became visible, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

I wrote this down in April 2021 after thinking about remote education tools from the vantage point of a kitchen table covered in charging cables. The better question, for me, was not the headline one but the one underneath it: what kind of life is this idea assuming? With technology, that is usually the first thing to disappear. Most conversations rush from capability to performance to scale while the livability question trails behind. Remote education tools seemed to me like a good way to keep the older question in view.

The part that mattered to me was not the mechanism people tended to highlight in public. The public version usually starts with whatever can be pointed to most easily: interface, model, robot, control surface. Social importance tends to arrive through side effects that stop feeling like side effects. They change pacing. They redraw the line between people who can stay broad and people who must become machine-readable. They redraw the boundary of neglect by routing more things through hidden workflows. Once you notice that shift, remote education tools stop looking like a gadget and start looking like a theory of everyday life with a power source attached.

I do not think atmosphere is a soft concern. It is where many technologies tell the truth about themselves. Some leave people feeling more capable and less managed. Others quietly convert daily life into a set of nudges, checks, and approvals. That effect may not show up in a benchmark chart, but it absolutely shows up in the lived experience of the system.

Looking back from April 2021, I could already feel my own position shifting. I kept thinking about an older post of mine, “On telepresence rituals under pressure,” from January 2021. In that earlier note I was still describing the surface appeal. I found myself looking past the applause toward the slower layer of maintenance, politics, sorting, and emotional obligation. I do not think that earlier optimism was wrong, exactly. Nothing about it was dishonest, it was simply the kind of optimism that arrives before enough contact.

The goal, to me, is not anti-technology purity. The better goal is to become harder to flatter. Admiration does not require silence about the world a tool is slowly constructing around itself. If that sounds like a modest ambition, good. Most of the sanest writing I know begins modestly. It starts by resisting the big story until the smaller one proves itself durable. That is a lesson I hear again when I reread “The problem with autonomous transit is usually the way optimism can hide a labor model.”

A final note to myself from April 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. What keeps my attention now is not the forecast but the burden redistributed onto the people closest by. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
