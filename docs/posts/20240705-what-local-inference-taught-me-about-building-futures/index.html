What local inference taught me about building futures

A post about local inference after the magic show, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

My notes on local inference began with a small irritation in July 2024. The agreement in the room was almost too smooth, and that smoothness made me wary. When a formulation travels instantly, I assume some awkward but important detail got left behind. In this case the omitted piece was how trust is built from boring details. What sounds abstract from altitude becomes concrete very quickly once you ask who maintains the system, who absorbs failure, and who benefits from early trust.

Local inference appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. Engineering is the easy half of the conversation because it turns so neatly into components, benchmarks, and demonstrations. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. A tool never arrives alone, it brings a sketch of its ideal user and a guess about the social order it belongs inside. If you pay attention, those assumptions are right there in the design. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the kinds of submission, rhythm, and expectation the system normalizes.

The most important distance was the one between the prototype environment and the wider public environment. Prototype conditions at least make the experimental nature of the work explicit. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. There is a big difference between testing on willing enthusiasts and asking ordinary people to inherit the result. It is one thing in a test setting and another in schools, offices, hospitals, housing, or transit where people inherit the result as environment. How trust is built from boring details becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

My taste for the subject stayed mostly intact, but my threshold for abstraction fell sharply. After writing “On memory systems while everyone tried to make software feel responsible,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. I trust claims about the future only when they can survive the messier terms of actual life. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. What matters is noticing when usefulness starts hardening into dependence and weirdness gets sold back to us as fate. Local inference seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually it arrives as a relationship pattern that turns ordinary before anyone has really consented to it.

A final note to myself from July 2024: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. At this point I read the future mainly through the demands a tool places on its closest users. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
