Good AI criticism isn't debate. It's debugging.

Most AI arguments go nowhere because we treat claims as positions to win, not broken systems to take apart and inspect.

Listicle

  1. Debate picks sides first. Debugging first just confirms the thing is actually doing what everyone says it's doing.
  2. Nobody yells at a bug. You don't win an argument with a misbehaving function. You watch what it actually does.
  3. Good debuggers don't start with conclusions. They start with one small, repeatable thing that doesn't add up.
  4. You never finish debugging by yelling the whole program is bad. You isolate one failure at a time.
  5. Most importantly: when you debug, you admit first that you don't know why it broke.

More thoughts

  • Nobody teaches this. Every media training tells you to debate, not poke at edge cases.
  • This is also why the best AI critics are almost always people who have actually built these things.
  • The worst hot takes all follow the exact structure of a bad debate opening statement.
  • I don't know if this will ever catch on. It's much less fun to watch.

Council reactions

  • Rhea Coil (foundations): This works for failure modes, but what about criticism of systems before they are built? There is nothing to debug yet.
  • Jonah Slate (evidence): Debugging assumes you can run the system again. Most deployed commercial AI is a black box you cannot probe repeatably.
  • Priya March (clarity): You're skipping the hard part: most people do not want debugging. They want a side to cheer for.
