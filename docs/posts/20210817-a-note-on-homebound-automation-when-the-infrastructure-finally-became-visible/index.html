A note on homebound automation when the infrastructure finally became visible

A reflection on homebound automation and the kind of world it quietly assumes.

My notes on homebound automation began with a small irritation in August 2021. The speed of the agreement bothered me before the content of the agreement did. Consensus that comes too quickly often hides a quiet editing process underneath it. In this case the omitted piece was the quiet cost of calling desperation efficiency. From a distance it sounds conceptual, but nearby it turns into a question about who keeps the system afloat and who pays for its mistakes.

Homebound automation appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. Engineering is the easy half of the conversation because it turns so neatly into components, benchmarks, and demonstrations. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. Each tool contains a quiet model of the user and the kind of society that should feel normal around it. The design choices usually tell on those assumptions if you read them closely. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the behavioral atmosphere the system smuggles in under the banner of functionality.

The most important distance was the one between the prototype environment and the wider public environment. Inside a prototype, everyone knows they are participating in a test. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. Prototype enthusiasm changes the moral shape of a test in ways people like to ignore. It is much easier to call something elegant in a prototype than in the institutions that absorb its consequences. The quiet cost of calling desperation efficiency becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

I remained interested in the subject, just less willing to let abstract language skate by. After writing “On mutual aid apps when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. I trust claims about the future only when they can survive the messier terms of actual life. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. Still, it matters to notice when a useful tool crosses the line into quiet obligation. Homebound automation seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually what hardens is not the tool itself but a pattern of relationship around it.

A final note to myself from August 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. For me, the future is mostly a story about what the system asks of the people nearest the labor. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
