What telepresence rituals taught me about building futures

A post about telepresence rituals while daily life was being rerouted through software, written against the temptation to make the future sound cleaner than it is.

My notes on telepresence rituals began with a small irritation in October 2021. The easy agreement around the big story felt like a warning, not a comfort. If everyone repeats the same polished line, I assume some important grit has already been swept away. In this case the omitted piece was how coordination can feel intimate and brutal at the same time. What sounds abstract from altitude becomes concrete very quickly once you ask who maintains the system, who absorbs failure, and who benefits from early trust.

Telepresence rituals appealed to me because it sat right on the border between practical engineering and cultural fantasy. People default to the engineering part because it hands them diagrams, figures, and a clean demonstration script. The fantasy part is harder, but it matters just as much. Buried inside every tool is an expectation about the person it is for and the society it assumes. The design choices usually tell on those assumptions if you read them closely. The question is never just whether a system works. It is also about the emotional weather and dependency habits the system leaves behind it.

What would not leave me alone was the mismatch between prototype conditions and the world outside them. Inside a prototype, everyone knows they are participating in a test. In public, people inherit the result as environment. That difference changes the moral burden of design. There is a big difference between testing on willing enthusiasts and asking ordinary people to inherit the result. It is much easier to defend the logic in a demo than in the places where people actually have to live with it. How coordination can feel intimate and brutal at the same time becomes sharper whenever a niche tool tries to skip directly into normal life without surviving a long middle period of explanation and repair.

The topic still held me, but my patience for abstract phrasing did not. After writing “On mutual aid apps when the world stopped pretending slack was infinite,” I found myself less willing to accept big words without a scene attached to them. If a claim about the future cannot survive contact with somebody's day, their tools, their fatigue, and their incentives, I do not expect it to last. That has become one of my small private tests. Ask the sentence to descend from altitude. If it lands gracefully, keep it. If it panics on impact, start over.

What I wanted, and still want, is not purity but proportion. Let the tools be useful. Let them even be strange. The key thing is to watch for the point where a useful thing stops feeling optional. Telepresence rituals seemed worth writing about because it carried that tension in plain view. The future is rarely a single invention. Usually what hardens is not the tool itself but a pattern of relationship around it.

A final note to myself from October 2021: the useful test is not whether a technology makes me feel early, but whether it makes the surrounding world more durable, more legible, and a little more generous. I spend less time on the forecast now and more on the burden the tool lays on nearby people. If that sounds less glamorous than prediction, good. The glamorous version is usually how we miss the part that matters.
